# Palm Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Setophaga palmarum  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–21 cm  
Average Weight: 8–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly more vibrant in coloration, particularly in the yellow crown and black eye stripe.  
Distinctive Features: Bright yellow crown, black eye stripe, and a long, often upright tail. Males have a more pronounced black throat patch.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, shrublands, and forest edges. During migration, they frequent backyards, parks, and second-growth habitats.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in eastern Canada (Ontario, Quebec) and the northeastern United States (New England). Winters in the southeastern U.S., the Caribbean, and northern South America (Colombia, Venezuela).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants, traveling from northern breeding grounds to tropical and subtropical wintering areas.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, spiders, and small arthropods. Occasionally consumes berries and seeds, especially during migration and winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and restless, often flicking their tails. Forages on the ground and in low vegetation, sometimes following army ants to catch insects.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of high-pitched, musical notes ("peter-peter-peter"). Call: A sharp, metallic "tik" or "chink."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, building a cup-shaped nest of twigs, grass, and moss. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge in 8–10 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in wintering and migratory stopover regions, pesticide use, and climate change impacts on food availability.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and aids in seed dispersal through fruit consumption. Serves as prey for larger birds and mammals.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Two subspecies exist: *Setophaga p. palmarum* (eastern) and *Setophaga p. hypochrysea* (western), differing in migration routes.  
- Known for tail-flicking behavior, a key field mark during identification.  
- Some populations migrate as far as the Yucatán Peninsula and northern South America.
