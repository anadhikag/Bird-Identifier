# White-breasted Nuthatch  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Sitta carolinensis  
Family: Sittidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 14–15 cm  
Average Wingspan: 19–22 cm  
Average Weight: 21–26 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males and females are similar in appearance, though males may exhibit slightly brighter plumage.  
Distinctive Features: Black cap and nape, white face and underparts, blue-gray upperparts, and a long, straight bill.  

---  

## Habitat  
Deciduous and mixed forests, especially those with mature trees for nesting. Also found in suburban areas with mature trees and shrubs.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Eastern and central North America, from southern Canada to the central United States. Populations extend westward to the Great Plains.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory; some northern populations migrate southward in winter, while others remain resident.  
Migration Pattern: Short-distance movements in response to food availability and weather.  

---  

## Diet  
Primarily insects, spiders, and other arthropods. Also consumes seeds, nuts, and suet, especially in winter.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Climbs headfirst down tree trunks, using its strong feet and stiff tail for balance. Known for caching food in bark crevices. Active and vocal, often visiting backyard feeders.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Loud, nasal "yank-yank" or "quank-quank" calls. Also produces soft, whistled "fee-bee" songs during breeding season.  

---  

## Breeding  
Nests in tree cavities or nest boxes, typically 3–20 m above ground. Clutch size: 6–8 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–13 days, shared by both parents.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and urbanization.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and aids in seed dispersal. Nest cavities may later be used by other cavity-nesting species.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Stores seeds in bark crevices and can remember their locations for months.  
- Uses its tail as a brace while climbing vertically or upside down on trees.  
- Frequently visits suet feeders, making it a common backyard bird in its range.
