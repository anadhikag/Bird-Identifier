# Northern Fulmar

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Fulmarus glacialis  
Family: Procellariidae  
Order: Procellariiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 40–45 cm  
Average Wingspan: 100–115 cm  
Average Weight: 600–1,200 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and have a more pronounced black bill tip.  
Distinctive Features: White body with grayish-brown upperparts, black bill with a yellowish tip, and a flight pattern resembling a petrel but with more rigid wings.  

---

## Habitat  
Open ocean, coastal cliffs, and rocky islands. Nests on steep cliff ledges and rocky outcrops.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the North Atlantic and North Pacific Oceans. Key breeding sites include Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, and coastal North America (e.g., Alaska, Canada).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Post-breeding, populations migrate to tropical and subtropical waters. North Atlantic birds travel to the Azores or Caribbean, while North Pacific birds reach the equatorial Pacific.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily fish, squid, and marine invertebrates. Scavenges for food, often following ships to feed on discarded fish scraps.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often seen in groups at sea. Flies with rapid wingbeats and can hover while searching for prey. Known to regurgitate food when threatened.  

---

## Vocalization  
Nasal, goose-like calls, often heard during the breeding season.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in colonies on cliff ledges. Lays one egg per clutch. Both parents share incubation (40–50 days) and chick-rearing duties. Chicks fledge at 50–60 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Plastic ingestion, oil spills, and climate change affecting prey availability.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates marine invertebrate and fish populations. Guano contributes to nutrient cycling in coastal ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Can drink seawater using specialized salt-excreting glands.  
- Lifespan up to 40 years.  
- Frequently follows ships to scavenge for food.
