# Lincoln Sparrow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Melospiza lincolnii  
Family: Passerellidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 13–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 19–21 cm  
Average Weight: 14–18 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more distinct chestnut crown and darker eye line; females are slightly duller.  
Distinctive Features: Chestnut crown, white eyebrow stripe, dark eye line, and a buffy supercilium.  

---

## Habitat  
Prefers dense, low shrubs and thickets, especially near water. Found in riparian zones, swamps, and overgrown fields.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across western and central North America, from southern Canada to northern Mexico. Winters in the southern United States, Mexico, and the Caribbean.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Migrates southward from breeding grounds to wintering areas in late summer and early autumn, returning north in spring.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily seeds, supplemented with insects during the breeding season. Forages on the ground and in low vegetation.  

---

## Behaviour  
Shy and secretive, often remaining hidden in dense cover. Active during the day, with males singing from perches to defend territories.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of clear, whistled notes, often described as "sweet-sweet-sweet" or "see-you-see-you." Call: A sharp, high-pitched "tik."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground or in low shrubs, lined with fine grasses and rootlets. Clutch size typically 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 11–12 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion, urban development, and wetland drainage.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seeds and insects consumed help regulate populations and disperse plant species. Nesting in dense vegetation supports biodiversity in shrubland ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males perform a "flight song" while hovering during courtship displays.  
- Often mistaken for the Song Sparrow due to similar size and coloration.  
- Nests are well-camouflaged to avoid predation.
