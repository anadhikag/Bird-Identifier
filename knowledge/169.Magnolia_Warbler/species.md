# Magnolia Warbler

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Setophaga magnolia  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 12–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 9–14 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a black throat and more vibrant yellow breast; females are duller with a grayish throat.  
Distinctive Features: Black and yellow face pattern, white wing bars, and a black throat in males.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in coniferous or mixed forests with dense understory; winters in mangroves, second-growth woodlands, and humid forests.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans eastern North America, from southern Canada to the northeastern United States. Winters in the Caribbean, Central America, and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance, traveling from North America to the Caribbean and Central/South America.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, particularly caterpillars, beetles, and flies; forages in the canopy by gleaning and sallying.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, often hanging upside down to reach prey. Shy and elusive, preferring dense vegetation.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A high-pitched, melodic series of notes, often described as "bee-bee-bay bee-bay." Call: A sharp, metallic "chip."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in coniferous trees or shrubs, building a cup-shaped nest 1–10 meters above ground. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–13 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds; collisions with buildings during migration.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as an indicator of forest health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Named after the Magnolia tree by Alexander Wilson in 1811.  
- Males and females resemble each other closely, but males have a distinctive black throat.  
- Migrates at night, using celestial cues for navigation.
