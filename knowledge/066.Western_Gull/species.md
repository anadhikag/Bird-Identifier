# Western Gull

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name:  
Larus occidentalis  
Family:  
Laridae  
Order:  
Charadriiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length:  
58–76 cm  
Average Wingspan:  
130–160 cm  
Average Weight:  
1.5–2.3 kg  
Male vs Female Differences:  
Males and females are similar in size; females may be slightly smaller.  
Distinctive Features:  
White head and underparts, dark wingtips with white-edged primary feathers, yellow bill with a red spot near the gape, and a robust build.  

---

## Habitat  
Coastal regions, including rocky shores, estuaries, and sandy beaches. Inland populations may inhabit lakes and reservoirs.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds along the Pacific coast of North America from Alaska to Baja California. Non-breeding populations extend to the Gulf of California and northern Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status:  
Partially migratory. Northern populations migrate southward in winter; coastal populations may remain year-round.  
Migration Pattern:  
Short-distance migrants, moving along the Pacific coast.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on fish, crustaceans, mollusks, scavenged food, and human refuse.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and opportunistic, often foraging in groups. Aggressive in defending nests and food. May steal food from other birds or humans.  

---

## Vocalization  
Loud, honking calls similar to other large gulls. Distinctive "kraa" or "kraak" sounds during social interactions.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on cliffs, rocky outcrops, or artificial structures. Clutch size is 2–3 eggs, incubated for 28–32 days by both parents. Chicks fledge at 5–6 weeks.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status:  
Least Concern  
Threats:  
Habitat degradation, pollution, human disturbance, and plastic ingestion.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates invertebrate populations and scavenges carrion, reducing disease risk. Serves as an indicator species for coastal ecosystem health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Hybridizes with Glaucous-winged Gulls (*Larus glaucescens*) in regions of overlap.  
- Known for tool use, such as dropping shellfish onto rocks to crack them open.  
- Longevity in the wild exceeds 20 years in some individuals.
