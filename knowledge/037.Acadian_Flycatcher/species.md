# Acadian Flycatcher  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Empidonax virescens  
Family: Tyrannidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 14–15 cm  
Average Wingspan: 23–26 cm  
Average Weight: 14–18 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males and females are similar in appearance, though males may exhibit slightly more vibrant plumage.  
Distinctive Features: Olive-green upperparts, yellowish underparts, pale eye ring, and a faint wing bar.  

---  

## Habitat  
Breeds in deciduous or mixed forests with dense understory, often near water. Prefers mature woodlands with a closed canopy.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding Range: Eastern North America, from southern Canada (Ontario, Quebec) to the Gulf Coast (Texas, Florida).  
Winter Range: Central and northern South America (Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador).  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Travels transcontinentally from North America to South America, with some populations wintering in the Caribbean.  

---  

## Diet  
Primarily insects, including beetles, flies, and caterpillars. Hunts by sallying from branches to catch prey mid-air.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Solitary and territorial during breeding. Often perches conspicuously to sing. Shy and elusive, preferring dense foliage.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Song: A sharp, clear "peet-sah" call and a distinctive "fee-bee" song.  
Vocalization Use: Used for territory defense and mate attraction.  

---  

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, building a cup-shaped nest of bark strips, leaves, and grass. Clutch size: 2–4 eggs. Both parents incubate and feed young.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation, forest fragmentation, and pesticide use.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as an indicator species for forest health.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- The "fee-bee" song is a key identifier, often described as a whistled duet.  
- Builds nests in a variety of structures, including trees, shrubs, and even buildings.  
- Migrates over 5,000 km annually between breeding and wintering grounds.
