# Barn Swallow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Hirundo rustica  
Family: Hirundinidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 17–19 cm  
Average Wingspan: 31–34 cm  
Average Weight: 16–22 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have slightly longer tail streamers and more intense blue-black upperparts.  
Distinctive Features: Blue-black upperparts, orange-red throat and underparts, long forked tail, and pointed wings.  

---

## Habitat  
Open habitats with cliffs, bridges, or buildings for nesting, including farmlands, pastures, and near water bodies.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Found across North America, Europe, Asia, and Africa. Some populations migrate to South America or sub-Saharan Africa.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory (some populations are resident in warmer regions).  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants; North American populations travel to South America, while Eurasian populations winter in sub-Saharan Africa.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily flying insects, including flies, beetles, and mosquitoes, caught in mid-air.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly social, often seen in flocks. Perform acrobatic aerial displays during courtship and foraging. Nest in colonies, building cup-shaped nests from mud pellets.  

---

## Vocalization  
Chirping, musical calls used for communication, courtship, and territorial defense.  

---

## Breeding  
Lays 3–7 eggs per clutch in a mud nest attached to vertical surfaces. Incubation lasts 14–15 days, shared by both parents. May raise multiple broods in a season.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural intensification, pesticide use, and reduced availability of nesting sites.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, particularly pests in agricultural areas. Indicator species for ecosystem health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Builds nests using over 1,000 mud pellets, often returning to the same site annually.  
- Recognized as one of the longest-distance migratory birds, with some individuals traveling over 22,000 km annually.  
- Frequently depicted in art and folklore as a symbol of good luck and spring.
