# American Goldfinch

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Spinus tristis  
Family: Fringillidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 21–26 cm  
Average Weight: 11–21 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have bright yellow bodies with black caps and bold black-and-white wing markings; females are duller with olive-yellow tones and less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Black-and-white wing bars, conical beak adapted for seed-eating, and a short, notched tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Open habitats including meadows, fields, gardens, and weedy areas. Prefers areas with abundant thistle and sunflower plants.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America from southern Canada to the northern United States. Winters in the southern United States and parts of Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in autumn; some southern populations remain year-round.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily seeds, especially thistle, sunflower, and aster. Occasionally consumes insects during the breeding season.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, often hanging upside down to feed. Forms large flocks outside the breeding season. Known for its energetic, undulating flight.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A musical, warbling "perk-ee-perk-perk." Call: A sharp, high-pitched "perk" or "seet."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, typically 1–6 m above ground. Clutch size: 4–6 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–13 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seeds dispersal agent for native plants. Controls insect populations during breeding season. Indicator species for healthy grassland ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The state bird of Iowa and New Jersey.  
- Molts twice yearly, losing its bright breeding plumage in autumn.  
- Relies heavily on thistle seeds, often visiting backyard feeders in winter.
