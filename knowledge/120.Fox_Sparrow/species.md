# Fox Sparrow

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Passerella iliaca  
Family: Passerellidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 15–16 cm  
Average Wingspan: 23–26 cm  
Average Weight: 25–35 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and have more vibrant plumage, especially the rusty crown and breast.  
Distinctive Features: Rusty crown, gray face, streaked underparts, and a bold eye ring.  

---

## Habitat  
Fox Sparrows inhabit dense underbrush in forests, thickets, shrublands, and overgrown fields. They prefer areas with abundant ground cover for foraging and nesting.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding ranges include Alaska, western and central Canada, and the northern United States. Wintering populations extend into the southern U.S., Mexico, and Central America. Some populations are resident in milder climates.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants, with some populations traveling from northern breeding grounds to southern wintering areas. Resident populations exist in parts of the Pacific Northwest and California.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily granivorous, feeding on seeds, fruits, and insects. Insects and spiders dominate the diet during the breeding season, while seeds and berries are more common in winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Fox Sparrows forage on the ground, often scratching with both feet simultaneously to uncover food. They are social in winter flocks but territorial during breeding. Nesting occurs on the ground or in low vegetation.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song consists of a series of clear, whistled notes arranged in a descending pattern. The call is a sharp, metallic "chink" or "chit."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are built on the ground or in low shrubs, lined with fine grasses and bark. Clutch size is 3–5 eggs, with an incubation period of 12–14 days. Both parents feed the young, which fledge after 10–12 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and land conversion, though populations remain stable.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Fox Sparrows contribute to seed dispersal and insect population control. Their foraging habits help aerate soil and spread plant seeds.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Fox Sparrows exhibit significant plumage variation among subspecies, ranging from pale to dark morphs.  
- They are one of the few sparrows that scratch the ground with both feet at once while foraging.  
- Some populations remain year-round in coastal regions of the Pacific Northwest.
