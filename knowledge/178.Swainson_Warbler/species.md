# Swainson's Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Liophyllosoma swainsonii  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 14–15 cm  
Average Wingspan: 20–22 cm  
Average Weight: 12–16 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have slightly darker plumage on the head and back; females are paler with less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Dark head with a white eye ring, yellow throat, and olive-brown upperparts; lacks the bright colors of many warblers.  

---

## Habitat  
Dense undergrowth in deciduous or mixed forests, especially near streams or swamps. Prefers thickets and brushy areas with leaf litter for foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the southeastern United States (Florida to Virginia). Winters in Central America (Mexico to Panama) and the Caribbean (Cuba, Hispaniola).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Travels from the southeastern U.S. to Central America and the Caribbean; routes include overland and coastal flyways.  

---

## Diet  
Insects and spiders, including beetles, caterpillars, and ants. Forages on the ground and in low vegetation.  

---

## Behaviour  
Secretive and skulking; sings from low perches. Territorial during breeding season. Solitary outside of breeding.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of clear, whistled notes resembling "fee-bee-oh, fee-bee-oh." Call: A sharp, high-pitched "tik."  

---

## Breeding  
Builds a cup-shaped nest on the ground or in low vegetation. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation period: 12–14 days. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss from deforestation and development; climate change impacts on wintering grounds.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and contributes to seed dispersal. Indicator species for healthy, undisturbed forest ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Builds nests in dense vegetation to avoid predators.  
- Rarely seen due to its secretive nature and habitat preference.  
- Named after English naturalist William Swainson.
