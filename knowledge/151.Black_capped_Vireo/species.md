# Black-capped Vireo

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Vireo atricapilla  
Family: Vireonidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 12–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 20–23 cm  
Average Weight: 9–14 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a black cap and white eyebrow stripe; females have a grayish cap and less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Black cap (males), white "spectacles," yellow throat, and olive back.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, mesquite thickets, and shrubby areas with low vegetation. Prefers habitats with dense undergrowth for nesting.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the southern United States (Texas, Oklahoma, New Mexico) and northern Mexico. Winters in Mexico and Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Travels from U.S. and northern Mexico to southern Mexico and Central America for winter.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, particularly caterpillars, beetles, and spiders. Forages by gleaning from leaves and branches.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, often hanging upside down to catch prey. Defends territory during breeding season.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A rapid, whistled "See-you, see-you, see-you" with a rising inflection. Calls include sharp, metallic chips.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in thorny shrubs or low trees. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, shared by both parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion, urbanization, and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, particularly caterpillars, which can damage crops and native plants.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Uses thorny plants for nesting to deter predators like snakes and mammals.  
- Males perform a "flight song" while hovering to attract mates.  
- Historically declined due to habitat fragmentation but has rebounded in some regions with conservation efforts.
