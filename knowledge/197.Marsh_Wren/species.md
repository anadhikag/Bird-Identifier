# Marsh Wren  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Cistothorus palustris  
Family: Troglodytidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 10–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 15–18 cm  
Average Weight: 10–14 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males may have slightly longer tails; otherwise, sexes are similar.  
Distinctive Features: Brown and white streaked back, white eyebrow stripe, short cocked tail, and a long, curved bill.  

---  

## Habitat  
Marsh Wrens inhabit freshwater and brackish wetlands, including cattail marshes, reed beds, and dense vegetation near water.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Found across North America, from southern Canada to the United States. Breeds in the northern U.S. and southern Canada; some populations remain in the southern U.S. year-round.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate south to the U.S. and northern Mexico for winter; southern populations are resident.  

---  

## Diet  
Primarily insects and spiders, including beetles, flies, and caterpillars. They also consume small crustaceans and seeds.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Territorial during breeding season. Males build multiple nests (up to 20) to attract females, though only one is used for breeding. They are active during the day and forage by probing reeds and mud.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Song: A rapid, musical series of notes resembling a "jingly" or "bubbling" sound. Call: A sharp "chink" or "tseep."  

---  

## Breeding  
Breeding occurs in spring and summer. Clutch size is 4–6 eggs, incubated for 12–14 days primarily by the female. Chicks fledge after 10–12 days.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Wetland destruction, pollution, and climate change affecting habitat.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Marsh Wrens control insect populations and serve as indicators of wetland health. Their nests provide shelter for other small species.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Males construct multiple nests, but only one is used for breeding.  
- They prefer to build nests in cattails or reeds, often in dense colonies.  
- Their songs vary regionally, with some populations having unique dialects.
