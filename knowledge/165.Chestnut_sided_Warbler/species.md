# Chestnut-sided Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Setophaga pensylvanica  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 8–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have brighter chestnut sides and a more pronounced yellow crown; females are duller with less distinct chestnut coloring.  
Distinctive Features: Bright chestnut sides, white underparts, yellow crown, and black streaks on the back.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in young, regenerating forests with dense shrubs and saplings; winters in tropical moist forests and second-growth habitats.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans eastern North America from southern Canada to the northeastern and midwestern United States. Winters in the Caribbean, Central America, and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrant, traveling from North America to tropical wintering grounds in Central and South America.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, including beetles, caterpillars, and flies; also consumes spiders and small invertebrates.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active, acrobatic forager that flits through branches and foliage. Uses flycatching and gleaning techniques. Defends breeding territories aggressively.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A high-pitched, musical series of notes often described as "sweet, sweet, sweet, I'm so sweet." Call: A sharp, metallic "tik."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in low shrubs or saplings; clutch size is 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 11–13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, collisions with buildings, and climate change impacts.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and contributes to seed dispersal through fruit consumption during migration.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males develop their full adult plumage at one year old, with younger males showing less vibrant colors.  
- Often found in mixed-species flocks during migration and winter.  
- Nest placement in low shrubs makes them vulnerable to ground predators.
