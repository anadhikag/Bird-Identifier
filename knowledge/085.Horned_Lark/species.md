# Horned Lark

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Eremophila alpestris  
Family: Alaudidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 15–18 cm  
Average Wingspan: 30–35 cm  
Average Weight: 25–45 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have more pronounced black facial markings and a brighter yellow crown during breeding season.  
Distinctive Features: Black and yellow crown feathers, long tail with white outer feathers, and a streaked, earth-toned plumage for camouflage.  

---

## Habitat  
Open grasslands, prairies, agricultural fields, and arid scrublands. Prefers short vegetation for nesting and foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Widespread across North America, from Alaska and Canada southward to the United States, with isolated populations in Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward for winter; southern populations are resident.  

---

## Diet  
Seeds, grains, and insects (especially grasshoppers and beetles). Diet shifts to more seeds in winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Ground-dwelling and terrestrial, often seen running rather than flying. Performs aerial courtship displays with rapid, zigzag flights.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of high-pitched, musical notes delivered in flight. Call: A sharp, metallic "tink" or "chink" for alarm.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground in shallow scrapes lined with plant material. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation: 11–13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion, urban development, and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seeds dispersers and insect population regulators. Their presence indicates healthy grassland ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Performs dramatic "sky-dancing" courtship flights at dawn.  
- Nests are often hidden in short vegetation to avoid predators.  
- Some populations remain year-round in southern regions.
