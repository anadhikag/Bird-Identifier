# Rufous Hummingbird

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Selasphorus rufus  
Family: Trochilidae  
Order: Apodiformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 8–9 cm  
Average Wingspan: 11–13 cm  
Average Weight: 2.5–5 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a bright rufous gorget (throat); females have a white throat with rufous sides.  
Distinctive Features: Small size, metallic green back, white underparts, and a long, slightly curved bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, forest edges, gardens, and shrublands. Prefers areas with abundant flowering plants.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in western North America from Alaska to the Pacific Northwest; winters in Mexico and the southern U.S.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Long-distance migrant  
Migration Pattern: Travels up to 2,000 miles from Alaska to Mexico, often crossing the Gulf of Mexico in a single flight.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily nectar from flowers (especially red and tubular blooms); supplements with insects and spiders for protein.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly territorial, especially around feeders. Capable of hovering and flying backward. Active during daylight hours.  

---

## Vocalization  
Emits high-pitched, metallic "pip" or "chit" calls. Males produce rapid, clicking notes during courtship displays.  

---

## Breeding  
Females build cup-shaped nests from plant down and spider silk, laying 2–3 eggs. Incubation lasts 16–19 days; fledging occurs 18–22 days post-hatching. Males do not assist in nesting.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, pesticide use, and climate change disrupting migration timing.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Key pollinator for many flowering plants, including those in alpine and desert ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Undertakes the longest migration of any hummingbird species.  
- Males perform a dramatic U-shaped courtship dive to attract mates.  
- Can enter torpor to conserve energy during cold nights.
