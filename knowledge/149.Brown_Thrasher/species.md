# Brown Thrasher

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Toxostoma rufum  
Family: Mimidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 25–30 cm  
Average Wingspan: 33–38 cm  
Average Weight: 55–80 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and have more vibrant plumage; females are duller.  
Distinctive Features: Brown upperparts with heavy black streaks, pale underparts with dark spots, long tail, and a distinctive upright posture.

---

## Habitat
Brown Thrashers inhabit dense, thorny thickets, shrublands, and the edges of forests, especially in the southeastern United States. They prefer areas with low vegetation for foraging and nesting.

---

## Geographic Distribution
The Brown Thrasher is found across the eastern and central United States, from southern Canada to northern Florida and western Texas. It is most common in the southeastern U.S.

---

## Migration
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate to the southeastern U.S. and the Gulf Coast for winter, while southern populations are largely sedentary.

---

## Diet
Brown Thrashers primarily eat insects, including beetles, grasshoppers, and caterpillars. They also consume fruits, berries, and seeds, especially during winter.

---

## Behaviour
These birds are territorial and often seen foraging on the ground, scratching the leaf litter with their feet to uncover food. They are known for their skulking behavior and can be elusive.

---

## Vocalization
The Brown Thrasher is renowned for its complex and varied song, which can include over 1,000 different phrases. It also produces sharp "chuck" calls and alarm notes.

---

## Breeding
Brown Thrashers build cup-shaped nests in dense shrubs or low trees. Clutch size is typically 2–5 eggs, and both parents share incubation duties. The nest is often lined with fine grasses and rootlets.

---

## Conservation
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to urbanization and agricultural expansion is a primary threat. Pesticide use also poses a risk by reducing insect prey.

---

## Ecological Importance
Brown Thrashers help control insect populations and contribute to seed dispersal through their consumption of fruits and berries. They are also indicators of healthy shrubland ecosystems.

---

## Interesting Facts
- Brown Thrashers are among the most vocal of North American birds, with a song repertoire that can exceed 1,000 distinct phrases.
- They are known to mimic the songs of other birds and even mechanical sounds.
- The species is the state bird of Georgia and Tennessee.
