# Canada Warbler

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Cardellina canadensis  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 16–18 cm  
Average Weight: 9–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more distinct black throat and chest band; females are duller with less pronounced markings.  
Distinctive Features: Yellow crown patch, black-and-white striped head, and a white eye ring. Males display a black throat and chest band.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in dense undergrowth of coniferous or mixed forests, often near streams or wetlands. Winters in humid montane forests in northern South America.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across eastern North America, from Canada to the northern United States. Winters in Colombia, Venezuela, and Ecuador.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance neotropical migrant, traveling from North America to South America via the Caribbean and Central America.  

---

## Diet  
Insects and spiders, foraged on the ground or in low vegetation. Occasionally consumes berries.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active ground-foraging, often seen hopping through leaf litter. Solitary except during breeding season. Singing is prominent during courtship.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A rapid, high-pitched trill or series of notes. Call: A sharp "tik" sound.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground, hidden in dense vegetation. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation: 12 days, primarily by the female. Nest: Cup-shaped, made of leaves, twigs, and moss.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, pesticide use, and climate change.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as an indicator species for forest health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Migrates over 10,000 km annually, one of the longest distances for a small bird.  
- Males perform a "flight song" during courtship, singing while fluttering up to branches.  
- One of the few warblers that forage extensively on the forest floor.
