# White-throated Sparrow  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Zonotrichia albicollis  
Family: Passerellidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 14–16 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–21 cm  
Average Weight: 20–28 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males and females are visually similar; males may exhibit slightly more vibrant head markings.  
Distinctive Features: White throat, black and white head stripes, and a yellow patch above the eye. Two color morphs exist: "tan" (brownish back) and "white" (grayish back).  

---  

## Habitat  
Prefers shrubby habitats, forest edges, thickets, and overgrown fields. Often found in areas with dense undergrowth for nesting and foraging.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across Canada and the northern United States; winters in the southern United States and northern Mexico.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Migrates southward in autumn, with some populations remaining in northern regions year-round.  

---  

## Diet  
Primarily granivorous, feeding on seeds, grains, and fruits. Insects and spiders are consumed seasonally, especially during breeding.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Ground-foraging birds that scratch through leaf litter for food. Social and often seen in flocks outside the breeding season.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Song: A clear, whistled "Oh-sweet-Canada-Canada-Canada," often repeated. Call: A sharp "tseep" or "chink."  

---  

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground in dense vegetation. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge at 10–12 days.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to land development and agricultural expansion.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Seed dispersal and insect population control. Serves as prey for larger predators.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Exhibits two distinct color morphs ("tan" and "white") that coexist in populations.  
- Monogamous pairs often remain together for multiple breeding seasons.  
- Their songs are studied in avian communication research due to their complexity and regional dialects.
