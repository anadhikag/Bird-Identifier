# Cliff Swallow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Petrochelidon pyrrhonota
Family: Hirundinidae
Order: Passeriformes

---

## Identification
Average Length: 13–15 cm
Average Wingspan: 30–34 cm
Average Weight: 16–24 g
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more distinct black mask and slightly larger size.
Distinctive Features: Dark blue upperparts, rusty throat and forehead, and a long, deeply forked tail.

---

## Habitat
Cliff Swallows inhabit open landscapes with cliffs, bridges, or other structures for nesting. They are commonly found near water sources and agricultural areas.

---

## Geographic Distribution
Cliff Swallows are found across North America, from southern Canada to northern Mexico. They also occur in parts of South America during the non-breeding season.

---

## Migration
Migratory Status: Migratory
Migration Pattern: Breeds in North America and winters in South America, particularly in Argentina and Chile.

---

## Diet
Cliff Swallows primarily feed on flying insects, including flies, beetles, and mosquitoes, which they catch in mid-air.

---

## Behaviour
Cliff Swallows are highly social and often seen in large flocks. They are agile flyers, performing acrobatic maneuvers to catch prey. They roost communally in large colonies.

---

## Vocalization
Their calls include a variety of chirps, trills, and whistles. The most common call is a sharp, repeated "pip-pip-pip."

---

## Breeding
Cliff Swallows breed in colonies, building cup-shaped mud nests under overhangs or on man-made structures. They lay 3–5 eggs per clutch, and both parents share incubation and feeding duties.

---

## Conservation
IUCN Status: Least Concern
Threats: Habitat loss due to human development and pesticide use affecting insect populations.

---

## Ecological Importance
Cliff Swallows play a vital role in controlling insect populations, particularly those that are pests to agriculture and nuisance to humans.

---

## Interesting Facts
- Cliff Swallows are known for their synchronized group flights, which help confuse predators.
- They are among the few birds that can sleep while flying, using unihemispheric sleep.
- Their nests are made of hundreds of mud pellets and can take several days to construct.
