# Tree Swallow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Tachycineta bicolor  
Family: Tyrannidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 15–17 cm  
Average Wingspan: 30–35 cm  
Average Weight: 18–25 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have more iridescent blue-green plumage on the back and crown; females are duller with less iridescence.  
Distinctive Features: Iridescent blue-green back, white belly, and a small, pointed bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Tree Swallows inhabit open woodlands, forest edges, parks, and areas with scattered trees near water. They rely on tree cavities or human-made nest boxes for nesting.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America from Alaska and Canada to the northern United States. Winters in the southeastern U.S., the Caribbean, and Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants traveling from breeding grounds in North America to wintering areas in the southeastern U.S., the Caribbean, and Central America.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily feeds on insects caught in flight, including beetles, flies, and mosquitoes. Occasionally consumes small fruits in late summer.  

---

## Behaviour  
Agile and acrobatic fliers, often seen in small groups. Nest in colonies or solitary, using tree cavities or nest boxes. Return to the same nesting areas annually.  

---

## Vocalization  
Vocalizations include a soft, musical "tsee-tsee-tsee" and a sharp "tik" call. Songs are often heard during courtship and territorial displays.  

---

## Breeding  
Lays 4–7 eggs per clutch, with both parents sharing incubation duties. Nests are lined with grass, feathers, and sometimes mud. Breeding season peaks in late spring to early summer.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and competition for nest sites with invasive species like European Starlings.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Tree Swallows help control insect populations, particularly mosquitoes, and serve as indicators of ecosystem health due to their reliance on open habitats.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Nest colonies can include dozens of pairs, especially in areas with abundant nest boxes.  
- Return to the same nesting sites year after year, often reusing the same cavity.  
- Use mud to line nests, which helps regulate temperature and deter parasites.
