# Mallard

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Anas platyrhynchos  
Family: Anatidae  
Order: Anseriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 50–65 cm  
Average Wingspan: 84–98 cm  
Average Weight: 0.9–2.5 kg  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a green head, chestnut breast, and iridescent blue secondary feathers; females are mottled brown with an orange-brown bill.  
Distinctive Features: Males display a white neck ring and a metallic green head; females have a distinctive orange-brown bill with a dark spot.  

---

## Habitat  
Wetlands, ponds, lakes, rivers, marshes, and urban water bodies. Prefer shallow freshwater habitats with abundant vegetation.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to North America, Europe, and Asia. Introduced to Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, and parts of South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory; some populations migrate seasonally, while others are resident in temperate regions.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in autumn; routes vary by region.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous. Feeds on aquatic plants, seeds, insects, small fish, crustaceans, and mollusks. Forages by dabbling (upending) or grazing.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often seen in flocks. Flies in V-formation during migration. Capable of rapid takeoff from water.  

---

## Vocalization  
Males produce a low, raspy whistle; females emit the characteristic loud "quack." Vocalizations vary with context (e.g., alarm calls, courtship).  

---

## Breeding  
Monogamous during breeding season. Nests on the ground near water, lined with down feathers. Clutch size: 8–13 eggs. Incubation period: 23–25 days by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, wetland degradation, pollution, hunting, and hybridization with domestic ducks.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Key seed disperser in wetland ecosystems. Prey for predators like foxes, raptors, and humans. Indicator species for wetland health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Hybridizes readily with other duck species, including the domestic duck.  
- Urban populations have adapted to human-modified environments, such as city parks and reservoirs.  
- Some populations remain resident year-round in regions with mild winters.
