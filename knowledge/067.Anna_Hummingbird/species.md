# Anna's Hummingbird

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Calypte anna  
Family: Trochilidae  
Order: Apodiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 10 cm  
Average Wingspan: 13 cm  
Average Weight: 3–4 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a red crown, red gorget (throat), and a green back; females have a greenish crown with some red on the throat, and a green back.  
Distinctive Features: Small size, iridescent red gorget in males, and a long, slender bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, gardens, parks, and riparian areas with flowering plants.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Western North America, from southern British Columbia and southern Alberta to Baja California, and east to western Texas and New Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate south in winter; some populations remain resident in milder climates.  

---

## Diet  
Nectar from flowers (especially eucalyptus, salvias, and manzanita), supplemented with insects and spiders for protein.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly territorial, especially around feeding sites. Known for hovering flight and rapid, darting movements. Males perform a U-shaped dive during courtship displays.  

---

## Vocalization  
High-pitched, squeaky "tik-tik-tik" calls and a soft, high-pitched trill.  

---

## Breeding  
Females build small, cup-shaped nests from plant down, lichen, and spider silk. Clutch size is 1–2 eggs; incubation lasts 14–19 days. Males do not assist in raising young.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss and pesticide use, though their adaptability to human-modified environments mitigates risk.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Pollinates flowering plants while feeding on nectar, supporting plant reproduction.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The male's courtship dive can reach speeds of up to 22 m/s.  
- The fastest hummingbird in the U.S., capable of hovering at 50 wingbeats per second.  
- Enters torpor at night to conserve energy, with body temperature dropping from 40°C to 18°C.
