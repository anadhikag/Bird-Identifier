# Yellow Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Setophaga petersoni  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 8–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males typically have a brighter yellow plumage and a black line through the eye; females are paler with a faint or absent eye line.  
Distinctive Features: Bright yellow body, black eye line in males, pointed bill, and a small, compact body.  

---

## Habitat  
Dense shrubs, trees, and thickets in wetlands, riparian areas, and gardens. Prefers areas with abundant insect life.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America from southern Canada to the northern United States. Winters in the southern United States, Mexico, Central America, and the Caribbean.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrant, traveling from breeding grounds to wintering areas in late summer and returning in spring.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, including caterpillars, beetles, flies, and spiders. Occasionally consumes berries and nectar.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and agile, flitting through foliage to forage. Often hangs upside down to reach prey. Social during migration but territorial during breeding.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of short, sweet notes, often described as "sweet-sweet-sweet" or "see-you-see-you." Call: A sharp, high-pitched "tik."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in shrubs or trees, building a cup-shaped nest of grass, bark, and plant fibers. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, pesticide use, and climate change.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as prey for larger birds and mammals. Acts as an indicator species for wetland health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males build multiple nests but only one is used for breeding.  
- Often mistaken for the Nashville Warbler but distinguished by the eye line.  
- Known for its vibrant yellow plumage, which intensifies during the breeding season.
