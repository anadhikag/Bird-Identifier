# Blue-headed Vireo

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Vireo solitarius  
Family: Vireonidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 13–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 21–24 cm  
Average Weight: 11–15 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more vibrant blue head and less yellow on the underparts; females are duller overall.  
Distinctive Features: Blue head, yellow throat, white spectacles, and olive back.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in deciduous or mixed forests with dense understory, often near water. Winters in tropical forests or second-growth habitats.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans eastern North America, from southern Canada to the northern United States. Winters in Central America and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance, traveling from North America to Central and northern South America.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insects, including caterpillars, beetles, and flies. Also consumes berries and fruits, especially during migration and winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and inquisitive, foraging by gleaning insects from leaves. Territorial during breeding season. Often joins mixed-species foraging flocks.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song is a slow, whistled series of notes: *see-by-see-by-see*. Calls include a sharp *chip* and a soft *whispery* *fee*.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, building a cup-shaped nest of twigs, moss, and lichen. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds; pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and disperses seeds through fruit consumption. Serves as prey for larger birds and mammals.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Mimics the songs of other birds, including woodpeckers and warblers.  
- Juveniles lack the adult’s bold head pattern and are more uniformly colored.  
- Known for its habit of “anting” by dropping insects onto ant trails to remove parasites.
