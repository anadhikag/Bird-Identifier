# Red-bellied Woodpecker

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Melanerpes carolinus  
Family: Picidae  
Order: Piciformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 20–24 cm  
Average Wingspan: 30–36 cm  
Average Weight: 30–50 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a red cap extending down the neck; females have a smaller red crown patch.  
Distinctive Features: Black and white barred wings, red belly (often hidden), and a chisel-like beak.  

---

## Habitat  
Deciduous and mixed forests, woodlands, suburban areas with trees, and forest edges.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to eastern North America, ranging from southern Canada to the Gulf Coast of the United States, and into northern Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate south in winter; southern populations are largely resident.  

---

## Diet  
Insects (beetles, ants, caterpillars), fruits, nuts (especially acorns), and occasionally bird eggs or nestlings.  

---

## Behaviour  
Territorial and often seen in pairs or family groups. Drums on trees to communicate and excavates nest cavities in dead or dying trees. Forages by drilling into bark to extract insects.  

---

## Vocalization  
Loud, rolling "kuk-kuk-kuk" calls and sharp "quarrel" sounds. Drumming is used for communication and territory defense.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in tree cavities, typically 3–20 m above ground. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, shared by both parents. Fledging occurs after 22–28 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, pesticide use, and competition with invasive species (e.g., European Starlings).  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, disperses seeds via fruit consumption, and creates cavities used by other species.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The red belly is often hidden and rarely visible in the field.  
- Stores food in tree crevices for later consumption.  
- Drumming patterns are unique to individuals and used for communication.
