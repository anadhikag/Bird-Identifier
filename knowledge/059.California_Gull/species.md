# California Gull

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Larus californicus  
Family: Laridae  
Order: Charadriiformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 43–48 cm  
Average Wingspan: 115–130 cm  
Average Weight: 300–500 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males and females are similar in size and plumage; females may be slightly smaller.  
Distinctive Features: White head and underparts, gray back and wings with black wingtips, yellow bill with a red spot near the tip, and dark eyes.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in freshwater lakes, rivers, and marshes, particularly in the Great Basin. Winters in coastal areas, estuaries, and urban environments.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range includes Nevada, Utah, Oregon, and California. Winters along the Pacific coast from California to Baja California, Mexico, and occasionally in the Gulf of Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Migrates from inland breeding grounds to coastal wintering areas; some populations remain resident in milder regions.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on insects, fish, small mammals, eggs, carrion, and human refuse. Often scavenges or forages in agricultural fields.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly social, nesting in dense colonies. Aggressive nest defenders. Flies in loose flocks with rapid, direct flight. Adaptable to human-modified landscapes.  

---

## Vocalization  
Loud, harsh, and repetitive calls, including a characteristic "kraa" or "krrr" sound. Vocalizations vary by context, such as alarm or courtship.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground in colonies, laying 2–3 eggs per clutch. Incubation lasts 25–30 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge at 4–5 weeks.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss from wetland drainage, pollution, human disturbance, and climate change affecting breeding sites.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, scavenges carrion to reduce disease spread, and contributes to nutrient cycling in ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Historically used in orchards to control insect pests, though this practice declined with pesticides.  
- Adapted to urban environments, often seen near landfills, parking lots, and agricultural areas.  
- Known for kleptoparasitism, stealing food from other birds like terns and gulls.
