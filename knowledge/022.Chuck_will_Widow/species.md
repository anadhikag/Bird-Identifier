# Chuck-willed Widow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Antrostomus carolinensis
Family: Caprimulgidae
Order: Caprimulgiformes

---

## Identification
Average Length: 28–33 cm
Average Wingspan: 66–76 cm
Average Weight: 100–180 g
Male vs Female Differences: Slight size differences; males may appear slightly larger
Distinctive Features: Large eyes, long wings, and a long tail with white outer feathers; mottled brown and gray plumage

---

## Habitat
Chuck-willed Widows inhabit open woodlands, desert scrub, and agricultural areas with scattered trees. They are often found near rocky outcrops or along roadsides.

---

## Geographic Distribution
This species is found across the southwestern United States, including Arizona, New Mexico, and Texas, extending into northern Mexico.

---

## Migration
Migratory Status: Partially migratory
Migration Pattern: Some populations migrate to Mexico or the southern U.S. for winter; others remain in their range year-round.

---

## Diet
Chuck-willed Widows primarily feed on insects, especially moths, beetles, and flying ants, which they catch in mid-air during nocturnal foraging.

---

## Behaviour
Nocturnal in nature, these birds are often heard but rarely seen. They are solitary and territorial, especially during the breeding season.

---

## Vocalization
The Chuck-willed Widow is known for its distinctive call, a series of whistled notes ending with a "chuck" sound, often repeated in a rhythmic pattern.

---

## Breeding
Breeding occurs from April to August. Nests are simple depressions on the ground, lined with leaves or twigs. Clutch size is typically two eggs, incubated for about 22–24 days.

---

## Conservation
IUCN Status: Least Concern
Threats: Habitat loss due to urban development and agricultural expansion

---

## Ecological Importance
As insectivores, Chuck-willed Widows help control insect populations, particularly at night. Their presence is also an indicator of healthy, open habitats.

---

## Interesting Facts
- Chuck-willed Widows are often mistaken for whippoorwills due to their similar appearance and calls.
- They have excellent night vision, allowing them to hunt effectively in low light.
- These birds are known to roost on the ground during the day, often in rocky or brushy areas.
