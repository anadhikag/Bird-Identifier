# Black Tern

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Chlidonias niger  
Family: Laridae  
Order: Charadriiformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 28–33 cm  
Average Wingspan: 66–76 cm  
Average Weight: 90–140 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and have more extensive black plumage during breeding season.  
Distinctive Features: Black head, neck, and upperparts in breeding plumage; white underparts; long, forked tail; and a slender, pointed bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in freshwater marshes, lakes, and wetlands with emergent vegetation. Non-breeding populations frequent coastal lagoons, estuaries, and open water.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America, Europe, and temperate Asia. Winters in South America, sub-Saharan Africa, and Southeast Asia.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Undertakes long-distance migrations, traveling from northern breeding grounds to tropical and subtropical regions for winter.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, small fish, crustaceans, and aquatic invertebrates. Feeds by skimming water surfaces or hovering before diving.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly agile flier; often seen in flocks. Nests colonially in marshes. Displays aerial acrobatics during courtship.  

---

## Vocalization  
Calls include sharp, high-pitched "kik" or "krrrr" sounds, often given in flight or during territorial disputes.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground in wetlands, using floating vegetation. Clutch size: 2–3 eggs. Incubation lasts 21–23 days, shared by both parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Wetland drainage, pesticide use, and habitat degradation. Some populations face declines due to climate change and human disturbance.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as an indicator species for wetland health. Supports aquatic ecosystems by preying on invasive species.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Migrates up to 16,000 km annually between breeding and wintering grounds.  
- Nests often float if water levels rise, adapting to seasonal flooding.  
- Feeds on invasive zebra mussels in some regions, aiding ecosystem balance.
