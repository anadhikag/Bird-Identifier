# Cactus Wren

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Campylorhynchus brunneicapillus  
Family: Corvidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 20–23 cm  
Average Wingspan: 25–30 cm  
Average Weight: 35–45 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males may have slightly more pronounced plumage; otherwise, sexes are similar.  
Distinctive Features: Long, curved bill; brown and white streaked plumage; white eyebrow stripe; tail with white outer feathers.  

---

## Habitat  
Cactus Wrens inhabit arid desert scrub, thornscrub, and cactus-dominated environments, including saguaro and mesquite habitats. They rely on dense vegetation for nesting and foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Found in the Southwestern United States (Arizona, New Mexico, Texas) and northern Mexico (Sonora, Chihuahua). Range is limited to desert regions with suitable vegetation.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Non-migratory  
Migration Pattern: Resident year-round within its range.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insectivorous, feeding on beetles, grasshoppers, and spiders. Also consumes seeds, fruits, and nectar opportunistically.  

---

## Behaviour  
Territorial and social, often seen in pairs or family groups. Builds nests in cacti or dense shrubs. Forages on the ground and in low vegetation, using its bill to probe for insects.  

---

## Vocalization  
Songs include a series of clear, whistled notes and harsh, rattling calls. Known for loud, repetitive vocalizations during territorial disputes and courtship.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are built in cacti (e.g., saguaro) or thorny shrubs. Clutch size is 3–4 eggs, with both parents incubating for 14–16 days. Chicks fledge after 14–18 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to urbanization, agriculture, and desertification.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and disperses seeds through fruit consumption. Nesting in cacti contributes to plant health by deterring herbivores.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The Cactus Wren is the largest wren in North America.  
- It is the state bird of Arizona.  
- Known for its aggressive defense of nesting sites, even attacking predators like snakes.
