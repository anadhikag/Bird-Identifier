# Least Auklet

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Aethia pusilla  
Family: Alcidae  
Order: Charadriiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 13–15 cm  
Average Wingspan: 25–28 cm  
Average Weight: 30–50 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Unknown  
Distinctive Features: Small size, black-and-white plumage, short thick bill, and a hunchbacked posture. Breeding adults have a black head with a white crescent behind the eye.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds on rocky coastal islands and cliffs in the North Pacific. Non-breeding season spent in open ocean waters.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding: Alaska (USA), Russia (Kamchatka), Japan.  
Non-breeding: Southern Bering Sea, North Pacific Ocean.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Breeds in northern Pacific; winters in southern Bering Sea and adjacent waters.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily feeds on small fish (e.g., capelin) and zooplankton (e.g., copepods, krill). Forages by diving from the surface.  

---

## Behaviour  
Colonial breeder, nesting in dense colonies. Uses crevices in rocks for nests. Social and highly vocal, often flying in tight flocks. Agile underwater diver.  

---

## Vocalization  
High-pitched, nasal calls, often described as "quacking" or "chittering." Used for communication in colonies.  

---

## Breeding  
Clutch Size: 1 egg  
Incubation Period: 28–30 days  
Nest Type: Unlined crevice in rocks, sometimes with sparse vegetation. Both parents share incubation and chick-rearing duties.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat degradation from introduced predators, climate change impacts on prey availability, and oil spills.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls populations of small fish and zooplankton. Serves as an indicator species for marine ecosystem health. Guano contributes to nutrient cycling in coastal ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The smallest member of the auklet family.  
- Dives to depths of up to 50 meters to forage.  
- Colonies can number in the thousands, with nests spaced just inches apart.
