# Scott Oriole

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Icterus parisorum
Family: Icteridae
Order: Passeriformes

---

## Identification
Average Length: 14–16 cm
Average Wingspan: Unknown
Average Weight: Unknown
Male vs Female Differences: Males are bright yellow with black on the head, back, and underparts. Females are duller, with olive-yellow plumage and less black.
Distinctive Features: Bright coloration in males, black hood, and contrasting yellow underparts.

---

## Habitat
Scott Orioles inhabit open woodlands, riparian areas, and desert scrub, often near water sources.

---

## Geographic Distribution
Scott Orioles are found in the southwestern United States and northern Mexico.

---

## Migration
Migratory Status: Partial migrant
Migration Pattern: Some populations migrate seasonally, while others remain resident in milder climates.

---

## Diet
Scott Orioles primarily feed on insects, nectar, and fruit, often visiting flowers for nectar.

---

## Behaviour
Scott Orioles are generally solitary or found in pairs. They are known to visit hummingbird feeders for nectar.

---

## Vocalization
Their song consists of a series of clear, whistled notes, often described as flute-like and melodic.

---

## Breeding
Scott Orioles build cup-shaped nests in trees or shrubs. Clutch size is typically 3–5 eggs, with both parents sharing incubation and feeding duties.

---

## Conservation
IUCN Status: Least Concern
Threats: Habitat loss due to urbanization and agricultural expansion.

---

## Ecological Importance
Scott Orioles contribute to pollination and insect control in their ecosystems.

---

## Interesting Facts
- Scott Orioles are closely related to the Hooded Oriole.
- They are known to nest in saguaro cacti in desert regions.
- The species was named after the American ornithologist Alexander Wilson.
