# American Crow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Corvus brachyrhynchos  
Family: Corvidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 40–50 cm  
Average Wingspan: 90–100 cm  
Average Weight: 300–400 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and heavier than females.  
Distinctive Features: All-black plumage, strong curved bill, and a robust, non-migratory build.  

---

## Habitat  
American Crows inhabit a wide range of environments, including forests, urban areas, farmlands, wetlands, and open woodlands. They prefer areas with abundant food sources and nesting sites.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to North America, the American Crow ranges from Alaska and Canada southward to Central America. It is common throughout the contiguous United States and parts of Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in winter, while southern populations are largely sedentary.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on insects, seeds, fruits, small mammals, eggs, and carrion. They also scavenge and may prey on nestlings of other birds.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly intelligent and social, American Crows exhibit complex problem-solving skills, tool use, and cooperative behaviors. They are known to travel in groups, cache food, and display curiosity toward novel objects.  

---

## Vocalization  
Primary call is a loud, harsh "caw-caw." Additional vocalizations include softer croaks, rattles, and mimicry of other sounds.  

---

## Breeding  
Monogamous pairs build large stick nests in trees or on human-made structures. Clutch size is 3–9 eggs, with incubation lasting 18–19 days. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, collisions with vehicles, and pesticide exposure.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
American Crows play a critical role in ecosystems by controlling insect populations, dispersing seeds, and scavenging carrion, which reduces disease transmission.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Can recognize individual human faces and remember them for years.  
- Use tools such as sticks to extract insects from tree bark.  
- Engage in "funeral" behaviors, gathering around dead crows to vocalize and investigate.
