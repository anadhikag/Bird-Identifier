# Cedar Waxwing

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Bombycilla cedrorum  
Family: Bombycillidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 16–18 cm  
Average Wingspan: 28–34 cm  
Average Weight: 18–30 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Minimal; males have slightly longer tail feathers.  
Distinctive Features: Black facial mask, yellow tail tips, and waxy red-tipped secondary feathers.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, forest edges, orchards, and urban areas with fruit-bearing trees.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across Canada, the northern United States, and the Great Lakes region. Winters in the southern United States, Mexico, and Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate south in winter; some individuals remain year-round in southern ranges.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily fruits and berries (especially cedar and juniper berries), supplemented with insects during breeding season.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly social, often seen in flocks. Known for synchronized movements and unique "dipping" drinking behavior.  

---

## Vocalization  
Calls include a high-pitched "bzzz" or "pip," and a softer "tsee" for communication.  

---

## Breeding  
Builds cup-shaped nests in trees or shrubs. Clutch size is 3–6 eggs, with incubation lasting 12–14 days. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, pesticide use, and collisions with vehicles or windows.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Key seed dispersers for fruit-bearing plants. Insect population control during breeding season.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The waxy red tips on their wings are secreted by a uropygial gland and used for courtship displays.  
- Cedar Waxwings can consume up to 100 berries in a single day.  
- They lack a stomach muscle for digesting seeds, relying on quick passage of fruit through their digestive system.
