# Prothonotary Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Protonotaria citrea  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–21 cm  
Average Weight: 9–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have more vibrant golden-yellow crown and face; females are duller with less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Bright golden crown and face, blue-gray back, and a long, slightly notched tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in wooded swamps, bottomland hardwood forests, and riparian areas with standing water. Requires mature trees for nesting and dense understory for foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the southeastern United States (e.g., Florida, Georgia, South Carolina) and along the Mississippi River Valley. Winters in northern South America (e.g., Colombia, Venezuela) and Central America (e.g., Costa Rica, Panama).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrant, traveling from North America to Central and northern South America. Some populations migrate as far as northern Argentina.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, particularly aquatic invertebrates (e.g., beetles, flies, spiders), and spiders. Forages by gleaning insects from leaves and branches or hawking them mid-air.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, flitting through trees and shrubs. Uses its long tail for balance while foraging. Nests in tree cavities or nest boxes, often near water.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A clear, whistled "sweet, sweet, sweet, I'm so sweet" with a rising inflection. Call: A sharp, metallic "chink".  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in tree cavities or nest boxes, laying 3–5 pale blue eggs with brown speckles. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to wetland drainage, deforestation, and nest competition with invasive species (e.g., European Starlings).  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations in wetland ecosystems. Serves as an indicator species for healthy, undisturbed bottomland forests.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The only North American warbler that nests exclusively in tree cavities.  
- Named for its golden crown, resembling the hat of a French clergyman ("prothonotary").  
- Relies on standing water for breeding; declines in wetland areas correlate with population drops.
