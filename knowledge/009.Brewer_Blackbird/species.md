# Brewer's Blackbird

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Euphagus cyanocephalus  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 20–23 cm  
Average Wingspan: 33–38 cm  
Average Weight: 55–85 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are glossy black with a purple sheen and a bright blue head; females are brown with streaks and lack the male's vivid coloration.  
Distinctive Features: Males have a striking blue head and black body; females have pale eyes and streaked plumage.  

---

## Habitat  
Brewer's Blackbirds inhabit open grasslands, agricultural fields, wetlands, and urban areas. They prefer habitats with scattered trees for nesting.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across western North America, from southern Canada to northern Mexico. Winters in the southern United States and Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory; some populations are resident in milder climates.  
Migration Pattern: Short- to medium-distance migrations from breeding grounds to wintering areas in the southern U.S. and Mexico.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily granivorous, feeding on seeds, grains, and agricultural crops. Insects, fruits, and human food scraps are also consumed.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and often seen in flocks, especially in winter. They follow agricultural machinery to catch insects and forage in groups.  

---

## Vocalization  
Calls include a sharp "check" and a musical, liquid "prrrrr." Males produce complex songs during courtship.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are built in trees or shrubs, with 3–5 pale blue or greenish eggs. Males defend territories, and both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seed dispersers and insect controllers. Their foraging in agricultural areas helps manage pest populations.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males perform courtship displays with wing-fluttering and vocalizations.  
- They are common in urban and suburban environments.  
- Flocks may follow tractors to catch insects disturbed by plowing.
