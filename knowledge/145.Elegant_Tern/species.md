# Elegant Tern  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Sterna elegans  
Family: Sternidae  
Order: Charadriiformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 38–42 cm  
Average Wingspan: 90–100 cm  
Average Weight: 150–200 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and may exhibit darker plumage during breeding season.  
Distinctive Features: Black cap extending to the nape, white face, and a long, slender red-orange bill with a black tip.  

---  

## Habitat  
Elegant Terns inhabit coastal environments, including sandy beaches, estuaries, and lagoons. They prefer open, flat areas for nesting and forage in shallow marine waters.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Found along the Pacific coast of the Americas, from northern California and Baja California to Peru. Breeding colonies are concentrated in Mexico and the southwestern United States.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Breeds in northern regions (e.g., California) and winters along the coasts of Mexico and Peru.  

---  

## Diet  
Primarily feeds on small fish (e.g., anchovies, sardines) and marine invertebrates. Forages by plunge-diving or skimming the water’s surface.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Colonial nesters, often forming large breeding groups. Display aerial acrobatics while hunting and use coordinated flight patterns to herd prey.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Emit high-pitched, shrill calls during courtship and territorial disputes.  

---  

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground, creating shallow scrapes lined with pebbles or shells. Clutch size is typically 2–3 eggs, with both parents sharing incubation duties.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to coastal development, human disturbance, and climate change impacts on nesting sites.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates populations of small fish and invertebrates. Their nesting colonies contribute to nutrient cycling in coastal ecosystems.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Performs long-distance migrations, traveling thousands of kilometers annually.  
- Nests are vulnerable to flooding, prompting colonies to select elevated or well-drained sites.  
- Juveniles lack the black cap of adults and have a more mottled plumage pattern.
