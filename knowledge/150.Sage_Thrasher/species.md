# Sage Thrasher

## Basic Information
Scientific Name:  
Saxicola rubetra  
Family:  
Mimidae  
Order:  
Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length:  
8.5 inches (21.5 cm)  
Average Wingspan:  
12 inches (30.5 cm)  
Average Weight:  
1.2 ounces (34 g)  
Male vs Female Differences:  
Unknown  
Distinctive Features:  
Brown upperparts with dark streaks, pale underparts, long tail, and a distinctive, fluty song.  

---

## Habitat  
Sagebrush steppe, shrublands, and open woodlands with low vegetation.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Western North America, from southern Canada through the central and southwestern United States.  

---

## Migration
Migratory Status:  
Partially migratory  
Migration Pattern:  
Southern populations are largely sedentary, while northern populations migrate to the southwestern U.S. and northern Mexico for winter.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, spiders, and other small invertebrates, with some fruit consumption seasonally.  

---

## Behaviour  
Solitary and territorial during breeding season. Often perches on low shrubs and sings from elevated vantage points.  

---

## Vocalization  
The Sage Thrasher is known for its complex, flute-like song, which includes mimicry of other birds and mechanical sounds.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are built in low shrubs or on the ground, with 2–4 eggs laid per clutch. Incubation and parental care are shared between both sexes.  

---

## Conservation
IUCN Status:  
Least Concern  
Threats:  
Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion, urban development, and sagebrush destruction.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Plays a role in controlling insect populations and serves as an indicator species for the health of sagebrush ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts
- The Sage Thrasher is one of the few thrashers that sings from the ground rather than from elevated perches.  
- It is known for its ability to mimic a wide range of bird calls and even mechanical sounds.  
- The species is closely associated with sagebrush habitats, making it vulnerable to land-use changes.
