# Bewick's Wren

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Thryomanes bewickii  
Family: Troglodytidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 10.5–11.5 cm  
Average Wingspan: 13–15 cm  
Average Weight: 9–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and may have a more pronounced white supercilium.  
Distinctive Features: Long, upright tail; white supercilium; brown back with faint streaks; pale yellow throat and belly.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, shrublands, suburban gardens, and forest edges. Avoids dense forests and arid deserts.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to North America, ranging from southern Canada to northern Mexico. Breeds in western and central United States; winters in southern U.S., Mexico, and Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward to winter in the southern U.S. and Mexico; some southern populations remain resident.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, spiders, beetles, and other small arthropods. Occasionally consumes seeds in winter. Forages on the ground and in low vegetation.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and inquisitive, often flicking its tail and hopping rapidly. Solitary or in pairs outside the breeding season. Displays aggressive territoriality during nesting.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of clear, musical notes, often described as a trill or a descending phrase. Calls include sharp "teakettle" or "chink" sounds.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in cavities (e.g., woodpecker holes, nest boxes) or dense shrubs. Clutch size: 4–7 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, competition with invasive species (e.g., House Wren), and brood parasitism by Brown-headed Cowbirds.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations in ecosystems. Serves as prey for raptors and snakes. Nest cavities may later be used by other cavity-nesting species.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males sing complex, variable songs to defend territory and attract mates.  
- Nests are often built in unusual locations, including mailboxes, clothespins, or abandoned vehicles.  
- Brood parasitism by Brown-headed Cowbirds can reduce breeding success in some populations.
