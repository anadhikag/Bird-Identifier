# Hooded Oriole

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Icterus cucullatus  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 18–20 cm  
Average Wingspan: 25–30 cm  
Average Weight: 20–30 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a striking black hood, orange body, and white wing bars; females are duller with yellowish tones and a black eye stripe.  
Distinctive Features: Bold black-and-orange plumage in males; females exhibit a yellowish base color with contrasting black markings.  

---

## Habitat  
Prefers open woodlands, riparian zones, and suburban gardens with mature trees. Avoids dense forests.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the southwestern United States (Arizona, New Mexico), northern Mexico, and parts of Central America. Winters in Central and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Migrates from breeding grounds in North America to wintering areas in Central and South America, typically between September and March.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insects (e.g., beetles, caterpillars), supplemented with fruits, nectar, and cactus pulp. Known to pierce cactus fruits to access nectar.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and agile, flitting between branches while foraging. Builds hanging, woven nests in trees or shrubs. Solitary or in small groups outside the breeding season.  

---

## Vocalization  
Produces melodic whistles, mimicry of other birds, and sharp calls. Males sing complex songs during courtship.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are woven, pendant-shaped structures made of grasses and plant fibers. Females lay 3–5 pale blue or greenish eggs with brown spots. Incubation lasts 12–14 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in wintering grounds and breeding areas due to deforestation and urbanization.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and disperses seeds through fruit consumption. Pollinates plants while feeding on nectar.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Uses its beak to pierce cactus fruits to drink nectar.  
- Males perform wing-flashing displays during courtship.  
- Known for mimicking the calls of other bird species.
