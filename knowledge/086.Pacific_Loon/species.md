# Pacific Loon

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Gavia pacifica  
Family: Gavidae  
Order: Gaviiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 55–65 cm  
Average Wingspan: 90–100 cm  
Average Weight: 1.5–2.5 kg  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and heavier; plumage differences are minimal.  
Distinctive Features: Black-and-white checkered back pattern, pale gray neck, and a sharp, pointed bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in freshwater lakes and ponds in boreal and subarctic regions; winters in coastal marine environments, estuaries, and brackish lagoons.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in Alaska, western Canada, and Siberia; winters along the Pacific coasts of North America (California to Washington) and Asia (Japan, Korea).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance, traveling from Arctic breeding grounds to temperate and subtropical coastal zones.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily fish, supplemented by crustaceans, insects, and aquatic invertebrates. Uses its sharp bill to spear prey while diving.  

---

## Behaviour  
Solitary and territorial during breeding; travels alone or in small groups during migration. Dives deeply (up to 100 meters) to forage. Awkward on land but agile in water.  

---

## Vocalization  
Emits low, hoarse calls, including a soft "hoo-hoo-hoo" and a sharp "yip." Less vocal than the Common Loon.  

---

## Breeding  
Monogamous pairs form during migration. Nests are built near water using plant material. Clutch size: 1–2 eggs. Both parents incubate for 26–28 days and care for chicks.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss from wetland drainage, pollution, and disturbance at breeding and wintering sites.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates aquatic prey populations and serves as an indicator species for wetland and coastal ecosystem health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Dives deeper than most loon species, reaching up to 100 meters.  
- The black-and-white checkered pattern on its back is unique among loons.  
- Prefers open water for takeoff and landing, often gliding across the surface.
