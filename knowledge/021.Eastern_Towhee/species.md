# Eastern Towhee

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Pipilo erythrophthalmus  
Family: Emberizidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 9–10 in (23–25 cm)  
Average Wingspan: 12–13 in (30–33 cm)  
Average Weight: 1.1–1.7 oz (31–48 g)  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a black head, back, and breast; females are similar but with a grayish head and less intense rufous sides.  
Distinctive Features: Rufous sides, white belly, and a prominent white tail patch visible during flight.  

---

## Habitat  
Dense underbrush in woodlands, thickets, shrubby fields, and overgrown pastures. Prefers areas with ample ground cover for foraging and nesting.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across eastern North America, from southern Canada (Ontario, Quebec) to the Gulf Coast (Louisiana, Florida). Winters in the southeastern U.S. and along the Gulf Coast.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant. Northern populations migrate southward; southern populations may be resident.  
Migration Pattern: Migrates at night, often in small flocks.  

---

## Diet  
Seeds, insects (beetles, caterpillars), berries, and small fruits. Forages by scratching the ground with both feet.  

---

## Behaviour  
Ground-dwelling and secretive. Often seen fluffing its tail and flicking its wings. Active during the day, with frequent dust-bathing behavior.  

---

## Vocalization  
Call: A sharp, clear "drink-your-tea" whistle. Song: A series of whistled notes, often ending with a trill.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground or in low shrubs. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation: 12 days by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and urbanization; fragmentation of shrubland habitats.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seed dispersal through consumption of berries and fruits. Controls insect populations by preying on larvae and beetles.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The red eyes of the Eastern Towhee are a key field mark for identification.  
- Males perform a "flight song" during courtship, singing while flying in zigzag patterns.  
- Known to use abandoned nests of other birds for roosting.
