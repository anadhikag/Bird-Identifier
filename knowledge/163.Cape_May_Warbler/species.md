# Cape May Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Setophaga tigrina  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 12–13 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 9–13 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have black and yellow plumage with bold black streaks on the back and white wing bars; females are more subdued with less black and a yellowish wash.  
Distinctive Features: Black facial mask, white wing bars, and black-and-yellow plumage pattern.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in coniferous forests (spruce, fir) in boreal regions; winters in tropical mangroves, second-growth woodlands, and scrub habitats.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding: Eastern Canada (Nova Scotia to Quebec).  
Wintering: Caribbean islands, northern Venezuela, and northern Colombia.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Long-distance migrant.  
Migration Pattern: Travels from eastern Canada to the Caribbean and northern South America.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, particularly caterpillars (e.g., spruce budworms) during breeding season; may consume fruit in winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active foliage-gleaners, foraging in coniferous trees. Social during migration but territorial during breeding.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: High-pitched, rapid series of notes ("tsee-tsee-tsee"). Call: Sharp "tik" or "chit."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in coniferous trees; clutch size 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12 days (primarily by the female). Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds; climate change impacts on coniferous forests.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations (e.g., spruce budworms) in boreal forests. Seed dispersal via fruit consumption in winter habitats.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Named after Cape May, New Jersey, where it was first described.  
- Males perform wing-vibrating courtship displays.  
- A key species in managing forest insect outbreaks.
