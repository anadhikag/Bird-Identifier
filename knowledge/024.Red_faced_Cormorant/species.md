# Red-faced Cormorant

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Phalacrocorax urial  
Family: Phalacrocoracidae  
Order: Suliformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 60–70 cm  
Average Wingspan: 100–110 cm  
Average Weight: 1.5–2 kg  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger with more vibrant plumage during breeding season.  
Distinctive Features: Bright red facial skin, dark plumage with a greenish sheen, and a hooked yellow bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Coastal rocky shores, islands, estuaries, and bays. Prefers areas with abundant fish and nesting sites on cliffs or trees.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the North Pacific, including the Aleutian Islands, Kamchatka, and Japan. Winters along the coasts of China, Korea, and Japan.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Moves from northern breeding grounds to temperate and subtropical coastal regions during winter.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily fish, squid, and crustaceans. Dives from the surface to catch prey, using webbed feet to propel underwater.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and colonial, often seen in groups. Perches on rocks to dry wings after diving. Agile swimmers and divers, capable of reaching depths of 3–5 meters.  

---

## Vocalization  
Produces grunts, croaks, and low-pitched calls during breeding season. Vocalizations are used for communication within colonies.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in colonies on cliffs or trees, building platforms of sticks and seaweed. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 25–30 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge at 6–7 weeks.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat degradation, pollution, and human disturbance. Vulnerable to oil spills and overfishing in coastal areas.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates fish populations and contributes to nutrient cycling in coastal ecosystems through nesting colonies.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Dives up to 30 meters to forage for fish.  
- The red facial skin is more pronounced in males during courtship.  
- Colonies can number in the hundreds, with nests spaced closely together.
