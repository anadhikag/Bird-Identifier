# Northern Cardinal  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Cardinalis cardinalis  
Family: Cardinalidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 8.3–9.1 in (21–23 cm)  
Average Wingspan: 9.4–12.0 in (24–30 cm)  
Average Weight: 1.5–1.7 oz (42–48 g)  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are vivid red with a black face mask; females are pale tan with red highlights on crest, wings, and tail.  
Distinctive Features: Crested head, black facial mask in males, conical beak adapted for seed-eating.  

---  

## Habitat  
Deciduous and mixed forests, woodlands, gardens, shrublands, and swamps. Prefers dense undergrowth for nesting.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Eastern and central North America, from southern Canada to northern Florida and Texas. Populations extend into the Caribbean and northern Mexico.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in winter; southern populations are largely non-migratory.  

---  

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on seeds, fruits, insects, and small vertebrates. Seeds and berries dominate in winter; insects in summer.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Territorial, especially during breeding season. Males sing to defend territory and attract mates. Known for "anting" behavior, where they rub plants with ants.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Clear, whistled songs including "what-cheer, what-cheer" and "birdie-bird." Calls include sharp "chip" notes and a rattling alarm.  

---  

## Breeding  
Nests in dense shrubs or low trees. Clutch size: 2–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 11–13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, pesticide use, and collisions with windows.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Seed disperser for native plants and insect population regulator. Provides food for larger predators.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Males and females engage in courtship feeding, with males offering seeds to females.  
- Known for "gift-giving" behavior, where males present females with food during pair bonding.  
- Northern Cardinals can raise up to four broods in a single breeding season.
