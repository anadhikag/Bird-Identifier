# Downy Woodpecker

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Dryobates pubescens  
Family: Picidae  
Order: Piciformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 14–17 cm  
Average Wingspan: 25–30 cm  
Average Weight: 21–28 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a small red patch on the crown; females do not.  
Distinctive Features: Small size, black and white plumage, white back, and a short bill.

---

## Habitat
Downy Woodpeckers inhabit a variety of wooded environments, including deciduous and mixed forests, woodlots, and suburban areas with trees.

---

## Geographic Distribution
This species is found across much of North America, from southern Canada to central Mexico, and along the West Coast to the Pacific Northwest.

---

## Migration
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in winter, while southern populations are largely sedentary.

---

## Diet
Downy Woodpeckers primarily feed on insects, especially wood-boring beetle larvae, ants, and caterpillars. They also consume seeds and berries, especially during winter.

---

## Behaviour
They are active and acrobatic, often seen climbing vertically up tree trunks and branches. They use their strong bills to excavate wood for food and nesting.

---

## Vocalization
Their most common call is a sharp, whinnying "pik" and a rolling, high-pitched "kr-r-r-ee" used in communication and during courtship.

---

## Breeding
Breeding occurs from April to July. Nests are excavated in dead or living trees, with 3–6 white eggs laid per clutch. Both parents share incubation and feeding duties.

---

## Conservation
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and nest site competition with other cavity-nesting species.

---

## Ecological Importance
Downy Woodpeckers help control insect populations, particularly those that damage trees. Their nest cavities also provide shelter for other wildlife species.

---

## Interesting Facts
- They are one of the smallest woodpeckers in North America.
- They can store food in their throat for later consumption.
- They use their stiff tail feathers for support while climbing trees.
