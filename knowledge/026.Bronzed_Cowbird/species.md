# Bronzed Cowbird

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Molothrus aeneus  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 18–21 cm  
Average Wingspan: 30–35 cm  
Average Weight: 30–45 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a metallic bronze sheen on the body and a black face with a bare, glossy black patch around the eyes. Females are duller, with a grayish-brown body and lack the metallic sheen.  
Distinctive Features: Metallic bronze plumage in males, bare black facial skin, and a slender, pointed bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Open grasslands, savannas, agricultural fields, and scrublands. Prefers areas with scattered trees for perching.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to South America, ranging from Colombia and Venezuela south to Argentina and Chile. Also found in the Galápagos Islands.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory  
Migration Pattern: Some populations migrate seasonally, moving to lower elevations or more temperate regions during the non-breeding season.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on seeds, insects, fruits, and occasionally scavenging. Known to consume the eggs of other birds.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often seen in flocks. Known for brood parasitism, laying eggs in the nests of other bird species.  

---

## Vocalization  
Calls include a series of churrs, whistles, and mimicry of other birds. Males produce a distinctive, metallic-sounding song during courtship.  

---

## Breeding  
Females lay eggs in the nests of host species, such as finches and warblers. Chicks hatch in 12–14 days and are raised by the host parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Acts as a seed disperser and insect controller. However, brood parasitism can negatively impact host bird populations.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males exhibit a unique courtship display involving wing-fluttering and vocal mimicry.  
- The species was historically linked to cattle in South America, following herds to feed on insects.  
- Juveniles resemble females but have a more mottled appearance.
