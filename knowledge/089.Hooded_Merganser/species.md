# Hooded Merganser

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Lophodytes cucullatus  
Family: Anatidae  
Order: Anseriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 38–46 cm  
Average Wingspan: 64–73 cm  
Average Weight: 0.5–0.8 kg  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger with a more prominent white crest bordered by black; females have a brownish crest and lack the male’s white neck patch.  
Distinctive Features: Crested head, serrated bill for gripping fish, and a black eye patch.  

---

## Habitat  
Wooded swamps, rivers, lakes, and coastal estuaries with abundant tree cavities for nesting. Prefers freshwater habitats with dense vegetation for cover.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across eastern North America, from southern Canada to the northern United States. Winters in the southeastern U.S., along the Gulf Coast, and in the Pacific Northwest.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in fall; some individuals remain in milder winter climates.  

---

## Diet  
Aquatic insects, small fish, crustaceans, and amphibians. Uses its serrated bill to grasp prey while diving.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and often seen in small groups. Skilled divers and strong fliers. Nests in tree cavities, sometimes using nest boxes.  

---

## Vocalization  
Males produce a loud, ringing "kyow" call; females emit a softer "kyu-ah." Courtship displays include head-bobbing and crest-raising.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in tree cavities near water, laying 6–12 eggs. Incubation lasts 30–35 days by the female. Chicks are precocial and capable of swimming shortly after hatching.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss (wetland drainage, removal of nesting trees), pollution, and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls aquatic insect populations and serves as an indicator species for wetland health. Nesting in tree cavities supports other cavity-dependent wildlife.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The male’s white crest is more pronounced than the female’s brownish one.  
- One of the smallest merganser species in North America.  
- Occasionally nests in colonies, a behavior rare among ducks.
