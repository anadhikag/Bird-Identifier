# Western Meadowlark

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Sturnella neglecta  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 9–11 in (23–28 cm)  
Average Wingspan: 13–15 in (33–38 cm)  
Average Weight: 42–72 g (1.5–2.5 oz)  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more prominent black V-shaped marking on the chest; females are similar but slightly duller.  
Distinctive Features: Bright yellow underparts with a black V on the breast, brown-streaked back, and a long, pointed tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Open grasslands, meadows, agricultural fields, and prairies. Avoids dense forests.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across western North America, from southern Canada to northern Mexico. Winters in the southern U.S., Central America, and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate south for winter; some populations are year-round residents in the southern U.S.  

---

## Diet  
Insects (grasshoppers, beetles), seeds, grains, and agricultural crops. Forages on the ground by walking or short bursts of flight.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often seen in flocks outside the breeding season. Perches on fence posts or low branches to sing.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of clear, flutelike whistles, often described as "flute-like" or "melodic." Calls include sharp, metallic notes.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground in grassy areas. Clutch size: 3–7 eggs. Incubation: 12–14 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion, urban development, and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and disperses seeds. Serves as an indicator species for grassland health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The Western Meadowlark’s song is often featured in birding contests for its melodic quality.  
- The black V on the chest is a key field mark distinguishing it from the Eastern Meadowlark.  
- Declines in some regions correlate with the conversion of native grasslands to monoculture crops.
