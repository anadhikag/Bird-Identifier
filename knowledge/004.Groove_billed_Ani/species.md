# Groove-billed Ani  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Crotophaga sulcirostris  
Family: Cuculidae  
Order: Cuculiformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 35–38 cm  
Average Wingspan: 55–60 cm  
Average Weight: 100–140 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Sexes are similar in appearance; females may have slightly shorter tails.  
Distinctive Features: Black plumage with a metallic purple sheen, curved and grooved bill, long tail, and rounded wings.  

---  

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, savannas, agricultural fields, and second-growth forests. Often seen perching on fences or low branches.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Found in Central America (Mexico to Panama) and northern South America (Colombia, Venezuela, Brazil, Argentina). Occasional sightings in southern Texas and southern Florida, USA.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory. Northern populations migrate to southern South America; tropical populations are resident.  
Migration Pattern: Migrates from northern ranges (e.g., Mexico) to southern South America (e.g., Argentina) seasonally.  

---  

## Diet  
Insects (grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars), fruits, seeds, and small vertebrates. Forages on the ground by walking or running.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Highly social; often seen in groups. Communal roosting is common. Known for "ground squirrel" movement while foraging.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Loud, rolling "krrr-r-r-r" call, often repeated in bursts. Also produces softer chattering and alarm calls.  

---  

## Breeding  
Builds large stick nests in trees or on man-made structures. Clutch size: 2–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 14–16 days, shared by both parents. Nestlings fledge at ~14 days. Cooperative breeding with helpers from previous broods is observed.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and agricultural expansion; pesticide use reducing insect prey.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and disperses seeds through fruit consumption. Nesting sites provide shelter for other species.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Mimics calls of other birds to deter predators.  
- Engages in brood parasitism by laying eggs in nests of other cuckoo species.  
- Nests are sometimes reused by other birds and mammals for shelter.
