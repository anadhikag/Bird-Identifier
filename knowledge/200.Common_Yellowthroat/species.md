# Common Yellowthroat

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Geothlypis trichas  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 11–13 cm  
Average Wingspan: 19–21 cm  
Average Weight: 10–14 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a distinctive black mask across the eyes and throat; females lack the mask and have a more buffy, less contrasting appearance.  
Distinctive Features: Bright yellow throat and breast in males; olive upperparts; white underparts.  

---

## Habitat  
Common Yellowthroats inhabit dense, low vegetation near water, including marshes, swamps, thickets, and overgrown fields. They also occupy brushy areas along stream banks and in agricultural landscapes with shrubs.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America from Alaska and Newfoundland to the central United States. Winters in the southern U.S., Central America, and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants traveling from North America to Central and South America. Some populations in the southern U.S. may be partially resident.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, including beetles, flies, caterpillars, and spiders. During winter, they supplement their diet with seeds and berries.  

---

## Behaviour  
Territorial during breeding season; males sing from perches to defend territory. Forages by gleaning insects from vegetation. Shy and cryptic, often remaining hidden in dense cover.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A rapid, repeated "wichity-wichity-wichity" with a rhythmic, mechanical quality. Call: A sharp "chick" or high-pitched "chirp."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are open-cup structures built low in shrubs or grasses. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to wetland drainage, pesticide use, and brood parasitism by Brown-headed Cowbirds.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates insect populations and serves as prey for larger birds and mammals. Their presence indicates healthy wetland ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The male’s black mask is a key field mark for identification.  
- Builds multiple nests in a single breeding season, though only one is used for breeding.  
- The name "yellowthroat" refers to the male’s striking yellow throat patch.
