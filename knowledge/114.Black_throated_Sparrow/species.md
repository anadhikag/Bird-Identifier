# Black-throated Sparrow

## Basic Information
Scientific Name:  
Amphispiza bilineata  
Family:  
Passerellidae  
Order:  
Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length:  
13–15 cm  
Average Wingspan:  
21–24 cm  
Average Weight:  
15–20 g  
Male vs Female Differences:  
Males have a distinct black throat and upper breast; females are duller with a grayish throat.  
Distinctive Features:  
Black throat in males, white eye ring, and a long, thin bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Black-throated Sparrows inhabit arid and semi-arid environments, including desert scrub, sagebrush, and open grasslands with low vegetation.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
This species is found in the southwestern United States and northern Mexico, ranging from southern California and Nevada through Arizona, New Mexico, and into Texas and northern Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status:  
Partially migratory  
Migration Pattern:  
Some populations are resident, while others migrate short distances to lower elevations during winter.  

---

## Diet  
Black-throated Sparrows primarily consume seeds, supplemented with insects, especially during the breeding season.  

---

## Behaviour  
These sparrows are generally solitary or found in small groups. They forage on the ground and are active during the day.  

---

## Vocalization  
The song is a series of short, musical notes, often described as a trilling or chipping sound. Calls include a sharp "tsee" or "tik."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests are built on the ground or in low shrubs, constructed from twigs, grass, and lined with fine plant material. Clutch size is typically 2–4 eggs.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status:  
Least Concern  
Threats:  
Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion and urban development.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Black-throated Sparrows contribute to seed dispersal and insect control in their arid habitats.  

---

## Interesting Facts
- They are well adapted to hot, dry environments and can survive without drinking water, obtaining moisture from food.
- Males sing from elevated perches to defend territory and attract mates.
- The species is often found in sympatry with other sparrow species in desert regions.
