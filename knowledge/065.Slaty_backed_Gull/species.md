# Slaty-backed Gull

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Ichthyaetus hemprichii  
Family: Laridae  
Order: Charadriiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 45–50 cm  
Average Wingspan: 115–130 cm  
Average Weight: 500–900 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and may have more pronounced plumage in breeding season.  
Distinctive Features: Dark slate-gray back and wings, pale gray head, red bill with a black tip, and white underparts.  

---

## Habitat  
Coastal areas, estuaries, lagoons, and mangroves. Often found near human settlements and fishing activities.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Widespread across the Indian Ocean and western Pacific, including coastal regions of Southeast Asia, Australia, and the Arabian Peninsula.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate to southern regions during non-breeding seasons; some populations are resident year-round.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on fish, crustaceans, mollusks, insects, and scavenged food. Frequently forages near human activity and fishing boats.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often seen in flocks. Aggressive when competing for food. Nests colonially on islands or coastal cliffs.  

---

## Vocalization  
Loud, resonant honking calls, often used during territorial disputes or courtship.  

---

## Breeding  
Breeds in colonies, laying 2–3 pale blue-green eggs with brown speckles. Incubation lasts 28–30 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge at 4–5 weeks.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat degradation, pollution, and human disturbance at nesting sites.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Plays a role in nutrient cycling by scavenging and controlling invertebrate populations. Acts as an indicator species for coastal ecosystem health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The Slaty-backed Gull is distinguished by its uniformly dark back, unlike many other gull species.  
- It frequently follows fishing vessels to scavenge discarded bycatch.  
- Colonies may contain thousands of nests, with aggressive territorial behavior during breeding.
