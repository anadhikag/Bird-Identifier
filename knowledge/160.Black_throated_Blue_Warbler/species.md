# Black-throated Blue Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Setophaga caerulescens  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 12–13 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 9–13 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a striking black throat and blue upperparts; females are olive-brown with a yellowish wash on the sides.  
Distinctive Features: Males display bold black-and-blue plumage, while females have a pale eye ring and yellowish flanks.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in dense, mature deciduous or mixed forests in the eastern United States and southern Canada. Wintering populations inhabit humid lowland forests, mangroves, and second-growth woodlands in the Caribbean and northern South America.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans from southeastern Canada to the northeastern and Appalachian regions of the U.S. Wintering range includes the Greater and Lesser Antilles, northern South America, and parts of Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Undertakes long-distance migration, traveling from North America to the Caribbean and northern South America. Crosses the Gulf of Mexico during fall migration.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insectivorous, feeding on caterpillars, beetles, flies, and spiders. Occasionally consumes berries during migration.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, foraging in the forest canopy by gleaning insects from leaves and branches. Territorial during breeding season; males sing vigorously to defend nesting areas.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A clear, rising "bee-bee-bay" followed by a buzzy trill.  
Calls: Sharp "tik" or "chip" notes during flight and alarm.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, building a cup-shaped nest of moss, lichen, and plant fibers. Clutch size is 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, climate change impacts, and collisions with buildings during migration.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations in forest ecosystems. Serves as an indicator species for forest health due to sensitivity to habitat fragmentation.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males and females exhibit striking sexual dimorphism, with males resembling "blue jays" in appearance.  
- Undertakes a nocturnal migration, often flying at high altitudes.  
- Prefers old-growth forests for breeding, making it vulnerable to logging and land development.
