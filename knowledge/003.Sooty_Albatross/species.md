# Sooty Albatross

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Phoebastria fusca  
Family: Diomedeidae  
Order: Procellariiformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 85–95 cm  
Average Wingspan: 200–230 cm  
Average Weight: 2.5–4 kg  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and have broader beaks.  
Distinctive Features: Dark sooty plumage, pale gray face, and a hooked, yellow-tipped beak.  

---

## Habitat  
Subantarctic and temperate oceans, breeding on remote, rocky islands with coastal cliffs.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds on islands in the Southern Ocean, including South Georgia, Macquarie Island, and the Crozet Archipelago. Non-breeding ranges extend into the North Pacific and Atlantic Oceans.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Undertakes long-distance movements between subantarctic breeding grounds and northern feeding areas, often traveling thousands of kilometers annually.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily feeds on squid, fish, and marine invertebrates. Occasionally scavenges on carrion and discards from fishing vessels.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly social, often seen in large groups at sea. Uses dynamic soaring to travel efficiently. Nesting colonies are noisy and active during breeding season.  

---

## Vocalization  
Emits low, guttural croaks and grunts during courtship and territorial disputes.  

---

## Breeding  
Lays a single egg annually in burrows or on cliff ledges. Both parents share incubation duties, which last 65–70 days. Chicks fledge after 120–140 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Vulnerable  
Threats: Longline fishing bycatch, plastic ingestion, climate change impacts on prey availability, and habitat degradation.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Plays a key role in nutrient cycling by transporting marine nutrients to terrestrial ecosystems via guano. Serves as an indicator species for ocean health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The Sooty Albatross has the largest wingspan of any albatross species.  
- Individuals can fly continuously for days without flapping their wings, using wind currents efficiently.  
- Some populations have lifespans exceeding 50 years.
