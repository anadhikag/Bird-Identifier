# Florida Jay

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Cyanocitta cayanensis  
Family: Corvidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 23–28 cm  
Average Wingspan: 38–43 cm  
Average Weight: 80–100 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males and females are similar in appearance, though males may be slightly larger.  
Distinctive Features: Bright blue plumage with a black mask around the eyes, a long tail, and a rounded blue crest.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, pine forests, and suburban areas with scattered trees, primarily in the Florida Panhandle.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Endemic to the Florida Panhandle, including the Apalachicola National Forest and surrounding regions.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Non-migratory  
Migration Pattern: Residency is year-round within their range.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous; feeds on insects, fruits, seeds, acorns, small vertebrates, and human-provided food scraps.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and inquisitive, often seen in pairs or small family groups. Known for tool use and caching food.  

---

## Vocalization  
Produces a variety of calls, including harsh "jays," mimicry of other birds, and alarm calls.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, with 2–4 eggs laid per clutch. Incubation lasts 17–18 days, shared by both parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to urban development, wildfires, and climate change.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and aids in seed dispersal through fruit consumption.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The only jay species endemic to the southeastern United States.  
- Collects shiny objects (e.g., bottle caps, coins) to line nests.  
- Known to mimic the calls of other birds and even human speech.
