# Yellow-headed Blackbird  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Xanthocephalon xanthocephalum  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 20–24 cm  
Average Wingspan: 30–35 cm  
Average Weight: 45–70 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have bright yellow heads, black bodies, and white wing patches; females are brown with streaked underparts and lack the yellow head.  
Distinctive Features: Bright yellow head in males, conical bill adapted for seed-eating, and black-and-white plumage in breeding males.  

---  

## Habitat  
Yellow-headed Blackbirds inhabit freshwater marshes, cattail wetlands, and agricultural fields, particularly in North America.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across central North America, from southern Canada to the central United States. Winters in the southern United States, Mexico, and Central America.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Migrates southward from breeding grounds in late summer to overwinter in warmer regions.  

---  

## Diet  
Primarily granivorous, feeding on seeds of grasses and weeds. Also consumes insects, especially during the breeding season.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, forming large flocks outside the breeding season. Males perform aerial displays during courtship.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Produces a sharp, metallic "check" call and a melodic, warbling song during the breeding season.  

---  

## Breeding  
Males build open-cup nests in marsh vegetation. Females lay 3–5 eggs per clutch. Incubation lasts 11–14 days, with both parents feeding nestlings.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Wetland drainage, agricultural expansion, and pesticide use.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Acts as a seed disperser and insect population regulator. Wetland nesting supports biodiversity in marsh ecosystems.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- Males defend small territories during breeding but nest in colonies.  
- Feeds on invasive plant seeds, aiding in their control.  
- Juveniles resemble females but lack the adult’s bold coloration.
