# Pelagic Cormorant

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Phalacrocorax pelagicus  
Family: Phalacrocoracidae  
Order: Suliformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 50–60 cm  
Average Wingspan: 90–105 cm  
Average Weight: 1.5–2.5 kg  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are slightly larger and have a more pronounced white cheek patch during breeding.  
Distinctive Features: Small size, dark plumage with a white cheek patch in breeding adults, and a hooked yellow bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Coastal rocky shores, cliffs, and islands. Prefers areas with abundant marine life for foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
North Pacific Ocean, ranging from Alaska and British Columbia to northern California, and from Japan to the Russian Far East.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in winter; some populations remain resident year-round.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily fish (e.g., herring, smelt), supplemented with squid and crustaceans. Uses pursuit diving to catch prey.  

---

## Behaviour  
Excellent diver, capable of reaching depths up to 60 meters. Often forages in small groups and perches on rocks to dry wings.  

---

## Vocalization  
Deep, guttural croaks and grunts, often heard during courtship and territorial displays.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in colonies on cliffs or rocky islets, building nests from seaweed, sticks, and feathers. Clutch size: 2–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 28–32 days, with both parents sharing duties.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Oil spills, habitat degradation, and human disturbance during breeding seasons.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates fish populations and serves as an indicator species for marine ecosystem health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Has a unique white cheek patch during breeding, distinguishing it from other cormorants.  
- Can dive to depths exceeding 60 meters using its webbed feet for propulsion.  
- Often seen "pruning" wings on rocky outcrops after diving to remove excess water.
