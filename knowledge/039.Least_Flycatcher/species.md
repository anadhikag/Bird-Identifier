# Least Flycatcher

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Empidonax minimus  
Family: Tyrannidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 11–13 cm  
Average Wingspan: 20–24 cm  
Average Weight: 7–11 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have slightly brighter olive-brown upperparts and paler yellow underparts compared to females.  
Distinctive Features: Small size, olive-brown upperparts, pale yellow underparts, faint eye-ring, and a short, squared tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in deciduous or mixed forests with dense understory, often near water. Prefers open woodlands and forest edges for foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans eastern North America, from southern Canada to the northern United States. Winters in Central America and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrant, traveling from breeding grounds to wintering areas in Central America and northern South America.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insects, including flies, beetles, and caterpillars. Forages by sallying from perches to catch prey mid-air.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, often hovering briefly to pluck insects from foliage. Territorial during breeding season; males sing to defend nesting areas.  

---

## Vocalization  
Call: A sharp, high-pitched "fee-bee" or "peter-peter." Song is a rapid, whistled "fee-bee" repeated in a rising pattern.  

---

## Breeding  
Builds a cup-shaped nest of twigs, grass, and moss in trees or shrubs. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, shared by both parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, pesticide use, and climate change impacts on insect populations.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, particularly pest species. Acts as an indicator species for forest health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The smallest flycatcher in North America.  
- Often perches on exposed branches to scan for prey.  
- Males sing continuously during the breeding season to attract mates and deter rivals.
