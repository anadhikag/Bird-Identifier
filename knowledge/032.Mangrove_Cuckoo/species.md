# Mangrove Cuckoo

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Coccyzus minor  
Family: Cuculidae  
Order: Cuculiformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 25–28 cm  
Average Wingspan: 46–50 cm  
Average Weight: 40–50 g  
Male vs Female Differences: No significant differences in plumage; both sexes have a long tail, dark eye, and white-tipped tail feathers.  
Distinctive Features: Slender body with a dark crown and back, pale underparts, and a long, graduated tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Mangrove Cuckoos inhabit tropical mangrove forests, coastal swamps, and dense secondary growth. They prefer areas with thick vegetation for nesting and foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Found in the Caribbean (e.g., Cuba, Jamaica, Hispaniola), Central America (e.g., Mexico, Costa Rica), and northern South America (e.g., Colombia, Venezuela).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Non-migratory  
Migration Pattern: Unknown (residents in their range year-round).  

---

## Diet  
Primarily feeds on insects, spiders, and small fruits. Occasionally consumes lizards and other small prey.  

---

## Behaviour  
Shy and elusive, often foraging alone or in pairs. Nests are built in dense vegetation to avoid predators.  

---

## Vocalization  
Produces a distinctive, rhythmic "ka-ka-ka" call, often repeated in a staccato pattern.  

---

## Breeding  
Lays 2–3 pale blue eggs in a flimsy nest made of twigs. Both parents share incubation duties, which last approximately 12–14 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to coastal development and mangrove deforestation.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Plays a role in controlling insect populations and seed dispersal in mangrove ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The Mangrove Cuckoo’s call is often mistaken for that of the Black-billed Cuckoo.  
- Nests are built low to the ground, sometimes just above water level.  
- Juveniles have a cryptic brown plumage that helps them blend into mangrove environments.
