# Eared Grebe

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Podiceps nigricollis  
Family: Podicipedidae  
Order: Podicipediformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 30–38 cm  
Average Wingspan: 58–66 cm  
Average Weight: 200–450 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males slightly larger; plumage differences minimal outside breeding season.  
Distinctive Features: Black ear tufts in breeding plumage; golden-yellow eyes; slender, pointed bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in shallow freshwater lakes, marshes, and ponds. Winters in coastal marine and brackish wetlands.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America, Europe, and Asia. Winters in the Mediterranean, Africa, southern United States, and parts of Southeast Asia.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants; travel in flocks from breeding grounds to tropical and subtropical wintering areas.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily aquatic invertebrates (insects, crustaceans), small fish, and plant material. Feeds by diving and surface skimming.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious; forms large flocks outside breeding season. Performs elaborate courtship displays involving synchronized swimming and head movements.  

---

## Vocalization  
Emits grunting, croaking, and whistling calls. Vocalizations increase during breeding season and territorial disputes.  

---

## Breeding  
Constructs floating nests of aquatic vegetation in shallow water. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Both parents incubate for 21–25 days; chicks are precocial.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to wetland drainage, pollution, and climate change.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates populations of aquatic invertebrates and small fish. Indicator species for wetland health and biodiversity.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Undergoes dramatic seasonal plumage changes, with breeding males developing striking black ear tufts.  
- Capable of diving to depths of 2–3 meters to forage.  
- Known for "grebe walks," where they run across water on their toes to escape predators.
