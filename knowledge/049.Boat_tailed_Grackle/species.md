# Boat-tailed Grackle

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Quiscalus major  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 38–43 cm  
Average Wingspan: 51–58 cm  
Average Weight: 200–300 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are significantly larger, with longer tails and more iridescent plumage; females are smaller and less glossy.  
Distinctive Features: Long, keel-shaped tail; males exhibit a striking black, iridescent plumage with a purple sheen.  

---

## Habitat  
Coastal marshes, wetlands, lakeshores, and urban areas with water sources.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to the southeastern United States, ranging from Florida and Texas northward to New Jersey. Populations have expanded northward into the Midwest and Northeast in recent decades.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in winter; southern populations are resident.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on insects, seeds, grains, aquatic invertebrates, and human-provided food scraps.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often foraging in flocks. Opportunistic feeders, known to scavenge and exhibit bold behavior at bird feeders.  

---

## Vocalization  
Loud, metallic "krrrr" calls and softer, varied vocalizations. Males produce a harsh, grating song during breeding season.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs near water. Males perform tail-fanning displays to attract females. Clutch size is typically 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 14–18 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Wetland loss, urban development, and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates insect populations and disperses seeds. Serves as an indicator species for wetland health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males have a wingspan longer than their body length.  
- Known to mimic calls of other birds and mechanical sounds.  
- Urban populations adapt readily to human-altered environments.
