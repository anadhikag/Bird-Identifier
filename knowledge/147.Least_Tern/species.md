# Least Tern

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Sterna antillarum  
Family: Sternidae  
Order: Charadriiformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 23–28 cm  
Average Wingspan: 51–58 cm  
Average Weight: 50–85 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Sexes are similar; males may be slightly larger.  
Distinctive Features: Black cap, white forehead, and a yellow bill (black in breeding season). Slender body with narrow wings and a deeply forked tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Coastal beaches, sandbars, gravelly shores, and inland lakeshores. Prefers open, sandy areas for nesting.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds along the Atlantic and Gulf Coasts of North America, the Caribbean, and parts of South America. Winters in coastal regions of South America, the Caribbean, and the Gulf of Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory; some populations are resident in tropical regions.  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants from North America to South America and the Caribbean.  

---

## Diet  
Small fish (e.g., minnows, silversides), insects, and crustaceans. Hunts by plunge-diving or skimming the water’s surface.  

---

## Behaviour  
Agile fliers; often seen in flocks. Nests in colonies, sometimes with other terns. Defends nests by dive-bombing predators or using distraction displays.  

---

## Vocalization  
High-pitched, sharp calls including a "pip" or "krek" sound, used for communication in colonies.  

---

## Breeding  
Lays 2–3 eggs in a ground scrape, often on sandbars or beaches. Incubation lasts 21–25 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge at 21–28 days.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, human disturbance, predation, and climate change impacts on coastal nesting sites.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Regulates small fish and insect populations. Serves as an indicator species for coastal ecosystem health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- The smallest tern in North America.  
- Some populations migrate over 10,000 km annually.  
- Nests on human-made structures like jetties and breakwaters in coastal areas.
