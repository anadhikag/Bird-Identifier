# Olive-sided Flycatcher

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Contopus cooperi  
Family: Tyrannidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 14–15 cm  
Average Wingspan: 25–28 cm  
Average Weight: 18–25 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a slightly darker back and more pronounced black mask; females are paler with less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Olive upperparts, white underparts, black mask extending from eye to nape, and a white tail with black edges when spread.  

---

## Habitat  
Open coniferous or mixed forests, especially near rivers, lakes, or wetlands. Prefers areas with snags or dead trees for perching.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America from Alaska to Newfoundland. Winters in northern South America, primarily in Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Long-distance migrant  
Migration Pattern: Travels from North America to northern South America, often flying nonstop over the Gulf of Mexico.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, particularly flying beetles, flies, wasps, and moths. Catches prey mid-air using sally-pounce foraging.  

---

## Behaviour  
Solitary and territorial. Perches on high, exposed branches to scan for prey. Active during the day, often seen hovering briefly before darting to catch insects.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A loud, whistled "Quick, three beers!" or "Pee-yew, pee-yew." Call: A sharp "pip" or "chack."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in coniferous trees or shrubs, building a cup-shaped nest of twigs, moss, and lichen. Clutch size is 2–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 14–16 days, shared by both parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, pesticide use reducing insect populations, and climate change impacts on migration routes.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, particularly forest pests. Acts as an indicator species for forest health due to sensitivity to habitat changes.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- One of the few tyrant flycatchers to catch insects in mid-air using a "hover-and-pounce" technique.  
- Migrates over 10,000 km annually, one of the longest migrations for a small bird.  
- The black facial mask is more pronounced in males and used in territorial displays.
