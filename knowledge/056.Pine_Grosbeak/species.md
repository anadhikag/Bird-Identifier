# Pine Grosbeak

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Pinicola enucleator  
Family: Fringillidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 20–24 cm  
Average Wingspan: 28–34 cm  
Average Weight: 30–50 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are predominantly red and black with yellow accents; females are grayish-brown with yellowish underparts.  
Distinctive Features: Thick, conical beak adapted for seed-cracking; males have a striking red body and black wings.  

---

## Habitat  
Coniferous and mixed forests, especially boreal and subarctic regions. Winter habitats include mountainous areas and open woodlands with fruit-bearing trees.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across North America (Alaska, Canada) and Eurasia (Scandinavia, Siberia). Winters in northern United States, southern Europe, and temperate Asia.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partially migratory.  
Migration Pattern: Irregular, with populations moving southward in winter depending on food availability; some remain resident in milder regions.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily seeds of conifers (e.g., spruce, fir) and berries. Summer diet includes insects and fruits.  

---

## Behaviour  
Social and gregarious, often seen in flocks outside breeding season. Non-aggressive, but may compete with other seed-eaters.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of whistled, musical notes. Calls: Soft "chur" or "chink" sounds; males sing more frequently during breeding.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in coniferous trees using twigs, moss, and lichen. Clutch size: 2–4 eggs. Incubation: 12–14 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss from logging, climate change impacts on coniferous forests, and collisions with vehicles/buildings.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seed dispersers for coniferous trees; help control insect populations in summer.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Can store food in throat pouches for later consumption.  
- Known to visit bird feeders in winter, especially for sunflower seeds.  
- Their diet allows survival in harsh, cold climates where other birds struggle.
