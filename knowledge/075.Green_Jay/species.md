# Green Jay

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Cyanocorax yncas  
Family: Corvidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 30–35 cm (12–14 in)  
Average Wingspan: 38–43 cm (15–17 in)  
Average Weight: 70–100 g (2.5–3.5 oz)  
Male vs Female Differences: Males and females are similar in appearance, though males may be slightly larger.  
Distinctive Features: Bright green plumage with black markings on the head and back, blue wing patches, and a long, graduated tail. The face has a black mask-like pattern, and the underparts are pale yellow.  

---

## Habitat  
Green Jays inhabit tropical and subtropical forests, including lowland evergreen forests, secondary growth, and forest edges. They also occur in human-modified habitats such as orchards and suburban areas.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
This species is found in Central America and northern South America. Its range includes Mexico, Central America (from Guatemala to Panama), Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and northern Brazil.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Non-migratory  
Migration Pattern: Green Jays are sedentary and do not undertake long-distance migrations.  

---

## Diet  
Omnivorous, feeding on fruits, insects, small vertebrates, eggs, and nestlings of other birds. They also consume seeds and may scavenge.  

---

## Behaviour  
Highly social and vocal, Green Jays are often seen in pairs or small family groups. They are intelligent, curious, and may cache food. Aggressive towards intruders near nests.  

---

## Vocalization  
Their calls include a variety of harsh, metallic "krrr" sounds, whistles, and mimicry of other birds. Alarm calls are loud and repetitive.  

---

## Breeding  
Breeding occurs during the rainy season. Nests are built in trees or shrubs using twigs, leaves, and vines. Clutch size is typically 2–4 eggs, with both parents sharing incubation and chick-rearing duties.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and agricultural expansion.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Green Jays contribute to seed dispersal and insect population control. Their predation on other birds’ eggs and nestlings may influence local avian dynamics.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Green Jays are known to use tools, such as sticks, to extract insects from crevices.  
- They exhibit complex social behaviors, including cooperative breeding in some populations.  
- Their vivid plumage and vocal mimicry make them popular subjects for birdwatchers.
