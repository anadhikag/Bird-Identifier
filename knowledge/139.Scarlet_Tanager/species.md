# Scarlet Tanager

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Piranga olivacea  
Family: Thraupidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 16 cm (6.3 in)  
Average Wingspan: 25 cm (10 in)  
Average Weight: 23 g (0.8 oz)  
Male vs Female Differences: Males are vivid red with black wings and tail; females are yellow-green with darker wings and tail.  
Distinctive Features: Males exhibit striking red plumage, while females have a contrasting yellow-green body with blackish wing bars.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in mature deciduous and mixed forests in eastern North America. Winters in tropical humid forests and woodlands in Central and northern South America.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans eastern and central United States and southeastern Canada. Wintering range extends from southern Mexico to northern Argentina.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Neotropical migrant  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migration from North to South America, with some populations traveling over 4,000 miles annually.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insects, including beetles, caterpillars, and spiders. Supplements with fruits and berries during non-breeding seasons.  

---

## Behaviour  
Solitary and active forager, often flitting through the canopy. Shy and elusive, with a tendency to remain hidden in dense foliage.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song is a high-pitched, musical "fee-bee-lee" or "fee-bee-see." Calls include a sharp "chink" and a soft "churrr."  

---

## Breeding  
Constructs a cup-shaped nest in trees, typically 10–25 m above ground. Clutch size is 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, collisions with buildings, and pesticide use.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations through predation and aids in seed dispersal via fruit consumption.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males undergo a dramatic molt from olive-yellow to red plumage after their first year.  
- Juveniles resemble females but have duller plumage.  
- Mimics the calls of other birds to deter predators.
