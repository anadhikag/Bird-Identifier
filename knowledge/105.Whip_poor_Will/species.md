# Whip-poor-Will  

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Antrostomus vociferus  
Family: Caprimulgidae  
Order: Caprimulgiformes  

---  

## Identification  
Average Length: 20–23 cm  
Average Wingspan: 48–51 cm  
Average Weight: 45–60 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males may exhibit slightly brighter plumage; otherwise, sexes are similar.  
Distinctive Features: Mottled brown and gray upperparts, white underparts with dark barring, long tail, and a distinctive "whip-poor-will" vocalization.  

---  

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, forest edges, and clearings with sparse understory. Prefers areas with scattered trees and leaf litter for nesting.  

---  

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across eastern North America, from southern Canada to the Gulf Coast of the United States. Winters in Central America and northern South America.  

---  

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Migrates southward to Central and northern South America in late summer/fall, returning north in spring.  

---  

## Diet  
Insects, primarily moths, beetles, and other nocturnal flying insects, caught in mid-air during low-level flights.  

---  

## Behaviour  
Nocturnal and crepuscular; roosts on the ground during the day. Highly cryptic, remaining motionless when threatened. Solitary except during breeding season.  

---  

## Vocalization  
Known for its characteristic repeated call: "whip-poor-will," often given at night. Additional calls include a soft "whip" or a sharp "chuck."  

---  

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground, laying 1–3 eggs in a shallow depression. Incubation lasts 14–16 days, shared by both parents. Chicks fledge at 16–20 days.  

---  

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation, pesticide use reducing insect prey, and collisions with vehicles.  

---  

## Ecological Importance  
Controls nocturnal insect populations, particularly moths and beetles. Acts as an indicator species for forest health.  

---  

## Interesting Facts  
- The species' name derives from its repetitive, melancholic "whip-poor-will" call.  
- Camouflage is so effective that adults often remain undetected even when observed.  
- Some populations in the southern U.S. are non-migratory, staying year-round in milder climates.
