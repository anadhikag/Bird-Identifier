# Blue-winged Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Vermivora cyanoptera  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 11–12 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 9–12 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more distinct black mask and brighter yellow crown; females are duller with less pronounced markings.  
Distinctive Features: Bright blue wings with white wing bars, a black mask through the eyes, and a yellow crown stripe.  

---

## Habitat  
Prefers shrubby thickets, young forests, and overgrown fields. Often found in early successional habitats and along forest edges.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in central North America, from southern Canada to the central United States. Winters in Central America, primarily in Mexico and northern Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Travels from breeding grounds in North America to wintering areas in Central America, typically migrating in flocks.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insects, including beetles, caterpillars, and spiders. May consume seeds and berries in winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and agile, foraging by gleaning insects from branches and foliage. Territorial during breeding season.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song is a high-pitched, whistled "bee-bee-bEE" with a rising inflection. Calls include sharp "tik" notes.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in low shrubs or on the ground in dense vegetation. Clutch size is 3–5 eggs, incubated for 12–14 days. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and land conversion, as well as collisions with buildings and vehicles.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and contributes to seed dispersal in forested ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Hybridizes with Golden-winged Warblers to produce Brewster’s and Lawrence’s Warblers.  
- Known for its distinctive "yellow-crowned" appearance and blue wing patches.  
- Often found in young forests, aiding in forest regeneration by preferring early successional habitats.
