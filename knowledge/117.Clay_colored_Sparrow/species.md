# Clay-colored Sparrow

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Spizella pallida  
Family: Passerellidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 12–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–21 cm  
Average Weight: 12–16 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more distinct grayish head and breast, while females are generally duller with a paler eye ring.  
Distinctive Features: Grayish upperparts, pale eye ring, and a faint wing bar. Males display a faint breast streaking during breeding season.  

---

## Habitat  
Open grasslands with scattered shrubs, prairies, and savannas. Avoids dense forests.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in central North America, from southern Canada to northern Mexico. Winters in the southwestern U.S., Mexico, and Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants, traveling from breeding grounds to wintering areas in fall and returning in spring.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily granivorous, feeding on seeds. Insects are consumed more frequently during the breeding season.  

---

## Behaviour  
Non-territorial outside the breeding season; often found in small flocks. Forages on the ground by scratching for seeds.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A series of short, clear, musical notes, often described as a "whispering" trill. Call: Sharp, high-pitched "tik" sounds.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests on the ground in grassy areas, constructed from grasses and twigs. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 11–13 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed the young.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion and urban development.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seeds dispersers and insect population regulators. Their presence indicates healthy grassland ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Prefers grasslands with low shrub cover for nesting.  
- Migrates in loose flocks, often joining mixed-species groups during winter.  
- Ground-nesting behavior makes them vulnerable to predation and trampling.
