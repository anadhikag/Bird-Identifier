# Purple Finch

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Haemorhous purpureus  
Family: Fringillidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 13–15 cm  
Average Wingspan: 20–23 cm  
Average Weight: 14–21 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males exhibit vibrant raspberry-red plumage on the head, chest, and rump; females are more subdued with streaked brown and faint red on the rump.  
Distinctive Features: Males have a distinct red coloration, while females display a dark eye line and white wing bars.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in coniferous and mixed forests; winters in deciduous woodlands, suburban areas, and agricultural regions.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans Canada and the northern United States; winters in the southeastern U.S., Central America, and the Caribbean.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate southward in winter; southern populations may remain year-round.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily seeds, supplemented by insects during the breeding season. Uses conical beak to crack seeds.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and acrobatic, often seen in flocks outside the breeding season. Visits bird feeders for sunflower seeds.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song consists of varied, musical warbles with repeated notes; calls include sharp "hee-hoo" or "pip-pip" sounds.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in coniferous trees, with 3–5 eggs laid per clutch. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss, competition with House Finches, and climate change impacts.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seed dispersal and insect population regulation.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males derive their red coloration from carotenoid pigments in their diet.  
- Often mistaken for House Finches due to similar plumage patterns.  
- Known to hold seeds in their feet while cracking them with their beaks.
