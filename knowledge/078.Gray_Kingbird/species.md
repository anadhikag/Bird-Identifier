# Gray Kingbird

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Tyrannus dominicensis  
Family: Tyrannidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 20–23 cm  
Average Wingspan: 38–43 cm  
Average Weight: 30–45 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a slightly darker gray cap and more distinct black eye stripe compared to females.  
Distinctive Features: Gray upperparts, white underparts, black cap, and a long, slightly notched tail.  

---

## Habitat  
Open habitats such as savannas, pastures, roadsides, and second-growth woodlands. Often seen perched on wires, fences, or low branches near water sources.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Native to the Caribbean, northern South America, and the Gulf Coast of the United States (e.g., Florida). Widespread in tropical and subtropical regions.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant.  
Migration Pattern: Some populations in northern ranges migrate southward to Central America or the Caribbean during colder months.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily insects, including flies, beetles, grasshoppers, and moths. Occasionally consumes small fruits or berries.  

---

## Behaviour  
Territorial and aggressive, especially during breeding season. Defends territory from larger birds. Perches and sallies to catch prey mid-air.  

---

## Vocalization  
Calls include a sharp, metallic "pip" or a series of short, whistled notes. Songs are varied, high-pitched, and melodic, often delivered from exposed perches.  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in trees or shrubs, or on man-made structures like buildings. Clutch size: 2–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 14–16 days, shared by both parents.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss from deforestation, pesticide use, and climate change.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as an indicator species for open-habitat health.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Known for dramatic aerial displays during courtship.  
- Aggressively mob predators, including hawks and snakes.  
- Frequently nests on human-made structures like billboards or buildings.
