# Field Sparrow

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Spizella pusilla  
Family: Passerellidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 12–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–21 cm  
Average Weight: 12–18 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a more distinct rufous cap and a black-and-white breast spot; females are similar but less vibrant.  
Distinctive Features: Rufous cap, white throat, black-and-white streaked breast, and a slender conical bill.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, forest edges, thickets, and overgrown fields with dense undergrowth.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Eastern North America, ranging from southern Canada (Ontario, Quebec) to the Gulf Coast (Louisiana, Florida), and westward to Texas and Kansas.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Partial migrant  
Migration Pattern: Northern populations migrate to the southeastern United States (Georgia, South Carolina) for winter; southern populations may remain resident.  

---

## Diet  
Seeds, insects (especially beetles, caterpillars, and grasshoppers), and berries.  

---

## Behaviour  
Shy and terrestrial, often foraging on the ground. Builds nests in low vegetation, and both parents care for young.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A clear, whistled "sweet, sweet, sweet, pretty, pretty." Call: A sharp "chip" or "seet."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in low shrubs or on the ground. Clutch size: 3–5 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to deforestation and agricultural expansion.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seeds dispersal and insect population control in their habitats.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Builds nests with a side entrance to deter predators like snakes.  
- Both parents feed nestlings and fledglings.  
- Sometimes called the "Field Lark" due to its preference for open habitats.
