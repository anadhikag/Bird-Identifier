# Golden-winged Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Vermivora chrysoptera  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 10–11 cm  
Average Wingspan: 16–18 cm  
Average Weight: 8–11 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a black face mask, yellow crown, and contrasting yellow wing patches; females are more subdued with a grayish face and less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Bright yellow wing patches (epaulets), black facial mask in males, and a white wing bar.  

---

## Habitat  
Open woodlands, early successional forests, shrublands, wet thickets, and forest edges. Prefers habitats with dense understory and low vegetation.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in eastern North America, from central Canada (Ontario, Quebec) to the northeastern and central United States (e.g., New England, Appalachian Mountains). Winters in Central America (e.g., Mexico, Costa Rica) and northern South America (Colombia, Venezuela).  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance, traveling from North America to South America for winter.  

---

## Diet  
Insects and spiders, primarily foraged in the understory of shrubs and trees.  

---

## Behaviour  
Territorial during breeding season. Performs courtship displays involving wing-flashing and song. Often forages in pairs or small groups outside the breeding season.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A clear, whistled "bee-tee-tee-tee-oh" with a rising inflection at the end. Call: A sharp "tik" or "chip".  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in low shrubs or on the ground in dense vegetation. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, primarily by the female. Both parents feed nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss from forest maturation, agricultural conversion, and hybridization with Blue-winged Warblers.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and serves as an indicator species for early successional habitats.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Hybridizes with Blue-winged Warblers to form Brewster’s and Lawrence’s Warblers.  
- Declines linked to loss of young forest habitats due to urbanization and fire suppression.  
- Sensitive to changes in forest structure, making it a key species for habitat management.
