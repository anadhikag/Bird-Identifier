# Bay-breasted Warbler

## Basic Information
Scientific Name: Setophaga castanea  
Family: Parulidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification
Average Length: 12–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 19–21 cm  
Average Weight: 10–14 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a black crown and throat, yellow crown stripe, and white cheeks; females are duller with a yellow crown and reduced black markings.  
Distinctive Features: Males display bold black and yellow plumage with white cheeks; females are more subdued with a yellow crown and faint black markings.  

---

## Habitat  
Breeds in coniferous or mixed coniferous-deciduous forests, especially in spruce-fir stands. Winters in tropical forests and mangroves in the Caribbean and northern South America.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeding range spans eastern Canada and the northeastern United States. Winters in the Caribbean, northern South America, and Central America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrant, traveling from North America to the Caribbean and northern South America.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, primarily caterpillars, beetles, and spiders. Forages in the forest canopy by gleaning and sallying.  

---

## Behaviour  
Active and agile, flitting through trees and shrubs. Often joins mixed-species foraging flocks during migration.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song: A high-pitched, rapid series of notes: "tsee-tsee-tsee." Call: A sharp, high "chip" or "seet."  

---

## Breeding  
Nests in coniferous trees, building a cup-shaped nest of moss, lichen, and plant fibers. Clutch size: 3–4 eggs. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, shared by both sexes.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in wintering grounds and during migration; climate change impacts on breeding and foraging habitats.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations, particularly caterpillars, in boreal and tropical forests. Acts as a seed disperser during migration.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males undergo two molts annually, developing breeding plumage in spring.  
- Feeds almost exclusively on spruce budworms during outbreaks in boreal forests.  
- Juveniles resemble females but have less distinct markings.
