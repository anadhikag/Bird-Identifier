# Brewer's Sparrow

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Passerella breweri  
Family: Passerellidae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 12–14 cm  
Average Wingspan: 18–20 cm  
Average Weight: 12–18 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have a gray crown, black eye line, and a yellow supercilium; females are duller with less distinct markings.  
Distinctive Features: Dark eye line, yellow supercilium, and streaked underparts.  

---

## Habitat  
Open sagebrush, grasslands, and scrub habitats in western North America.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds in the western United States from Washington to New Mexico. Winters in the southwestern U.S. and northern Mexico.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Migratory  
Migration Pattern: Short-distance migration from breeding grounds to wintering areas in the southern U.S. and northern Mexico.  

---

## Diet  
Primarily seeds, supplemented with insects during the breeding season. Forages on the ground.  

---

## Behaviour  
Ground-foraging, often in flocks outside the breeding season. Builds cup-shaped nests on the ground or low in shrubs.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song consists of a series of clear, whistled notes, often described as "pip-pip-pip." Calls include sharp, high-pitched chips.  

---

## Breeding  
Lays 3–5 eggs in a ground nest. Incubation is performed by the female for 12–14 days. Both parents feed the young.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss due to agricultural expansion, urban development, and invasive species.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Seeds dispersal and insect population control in arid ecosystems.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Named after John Brewer, a 19th-century British naturalist.  
- Often mistaken for similar sparrow species due to overlapping ranges and plumage.  
- Population trends are stable despite habitat fragmentation.
