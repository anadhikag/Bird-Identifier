# Orchard Oriole

## Basic Information  
Scientific Name: Icterus spurius  
Family: Icteridae  
Order: Passeriformes  

---

## Identification  
Average Length: 15–18 cm  
Average Wingspan: 23–26 cm  
Average Weight: 17–23 g  
Male vs Female Differences: Males have black heads, backs, and tails with orange underparts; females are yellowish with black markings and a less vibrant coloration.  
Distinctive Features: Males exhibit a striking black-and-orange plumage, while females have a more subdued yellowish hue with a black eye stripe.  

---

## Habitat  
Orchard Orioles inhabit open woodlands, riparian areas, orchards, and suburban gardens with scattered trees. They prefer habitats with dense foliage for nesting and foraging.  

---

## Geographic Distribution  
Breeds across the eastern and central United States, from Texas to New England, and into southern Canada. Winters in Central America and northern South America.  

---

## Migration  
Migratory Status: Neotropical migrant  
Migration Pattern: Long-distance migrants traveling from North America to Central and northern South America for the winter.  

---

## Diet  
Insects, fruits, nectar, and small berries. Diet shifts seasonally, with more fruit consumption during migration and winter.  

---

## Behaviour  
Territorial during breeding season; males sing from high perches. Build intricate, pendulous nests in tree forks using plant fibers and spider silk.  

---

## Vocalization  
Song consists of a series of whistled notes, often described as a rapid, liquid "fee-bee-lee" or "chink chink." Calls include sharp, metallic "chink" sounds.  

---

## Breeding  
Males attract females with songs and courtship displays. Females build nests and lay 3–5 eggs per clutch. Incubation lasts 12–14 days, with both parents feeding nestlings.  

---

## Conservation  
IUCN Status: Least Concern  
Threats: Habitat loss in breeding and wintering grounds, pesticide use, and climate change impacts on food availability.  

---

## Ecological Importance  
Controls insect populations and disperses seeds through fruit consumption. Nesting in orchards and gardens supports biodiversity in human-modified landscapes.  

---

## Interesting Facts  
- Males may engage in "fluffing" displays to attract mates.  
- Nests are often built in colonies, with multiple pairs nesting close together.  
- Attracted to backyard feeders offering jelly or oranges.
